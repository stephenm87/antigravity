North Africa · Occupied Territory

🌱 Western Sahara: Green Colonization

Morocco's renewable energy expansion in disputed territory
💻 Technology ⚖️ Equality 🌍 Environment
📋 Context

Morocco is rapidly expanding renewable energy infrastructure — massive solar, wind, and green hydrogen projects — in Western Sahara, a UN-classified non-self-governing territory occupied since 1975. Projects like the "White Dunes" 8 GW hydrogen facility near Dakhla and a 1,400 km electricity transmission line to Casablanca are scheduled for 2025-2027. Critics, including WSRW (Western Sahara Resource Watch) and the Polisario Front, call this "green colonization" — using the global climate agenda to entrench occupation without Sahrawi consent.

👥 Key Actors

🔍 Theory Lenses

🦁 Realism

Morocco uses renewable energy to consolidate territorial control and attract great-power recognition. Resource extraction = power projection.

🕊 Liberalism

International law (UN resolutions, EU trade rulings) should protect Sahrawi self-determination. Yet enforcement is weak — exposing limits of the liberal order.

🧠 Constructivism

"Green" energy is being socially constructed as progressive, masking colonial extraction. The label "sustainable" legitimizes what might otherwise be condemned.

📝 Exam Angle

Paper 2 Prompt

"To what extent does sustainable development require self-determination?" Use Western Sahara to argue that development without political consent is exploitation, not sustainability.

Paper 3 Connection

Q2b Policy: Recommend that the EU enforce its own Court of Justice ruling banning imports from Western Sahara by implementing origin-of-energy tracing for green hydrogen. Q3 Essay: Link Technology + Equality — how green tech can reproduce colonial power asymmetries.

Central America · Authoritarian Governance

⛏ Nicaragua: Minerals for Sovereignty

Chinese mining concessions covering 60% of national territory
🏚 Poverty 🛡 Security 🌍 Environment
📋 Context

Since restoring diplomatic ties with Beijing in 2021 and signing a free trade agreement in 2024, the Ortega-Murillo regime has granted over 500,000 hectares in mining concessions to Chinese-owned companies. By 2026, 16 Chinese firms hold over one million hectares. Gold exports reached $1.39 billion in 2024 (+20.1% year-on-year). The government has earmarked 7.1 million hectares — nearly 60% of national territory — for potential concessions, many overlapping protected areas and indigenous territories. A 2025 law amendment would allow resource extraction within protected areas.

👥 Key Actors

States

  • Nicaragua (Ortega-Murillo) — Granting concessions, weakening environmental law
  • China — Strategic investment partner; FTA signed 2024
  • United States — Imposed mining sanctions 2022, 2024

Non-State Actors

  • Zhong Fu Development, Thomas Metal, Global Group — Chinese mining MNCs
  • Fundación del Río — Environmental NGO raising transparency concerns
  • Indigenous communities — Denied free, prior, informed consent
🔍 Theory Lenses

🦁 Realism

Nicaragua trades sovereignty over resources for Chinese economic partnership — a survival strategy against US sanctions. It's rational self-interest by the regime, not the people.

🕊 Liberalism

The erosion of environmental protections and indigenous rights violates liberal norms of governance and accountability. The 2025 law amendment shows institutional capture.

🧠 Constructivism

"Development" is constructed as GDP growth via extraction, ignoring alternative models (community forestry, ecotourism) that indigenous groups advocate. Whose development counts?

📝 Exam Angle

Paper 2 Prompt

"Evaluate the claim that economic development always comes at the cost of environmental sustainability." Nicaragua shows how authoritarian governance can accelerate extractivism while bypassing civil society.

Paper 3 Connection

Q2b Policy: Recommend the ICMM (International Council on Mining and Metals) enforce free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) protocols for all member-state concessions. Q3 Essay: Link Poverty + Environment — how resource extraction deepens inequality while degrading ecosystems.

Caribbean · Failed State Dynamics

🏴 Haiti: R2P vs. Neocolonialism

Kenya-led MSS to Gang Suppression Force: intervention or occupation?
🛡 Security 🏚 Poverty ⚖️ Equality 🏥 Health
📋 Context

Haiti's security crisis escalated through 2024-2026 as armed gangs committed mass killings, sexual violence, and territorial control that the OAS R2P Special Adviser classified as crimes against humanity (May 2025). The UN authorized a Kenya-led Multinational Security Support (MSS) mission in October 2023; Kenyan officers arrived June 2024. In October 2025, the UN Security Council transitioned the MSS into the Gang Suppression Force (GSF) — a larger mission of up to 5,500 personnel. Kenya subsequently withdrew. Critics argue these interventions serve the "Core Group" (US, EU, Canada, France) rather than Haitian self-determination, perpetuating a cycle of external control.

📰 Key Sources — Differing Perspectives
Critical Perspective
"Haiti: Strong International Engagement Necessary to Ensure Rights-Based Security"
Human Rights Watch (HRW) · 2025
Read article on hrw.org ↗

Documents 5,600+ killed in 2024 after MSS deployment. Raises vetting and accountability concerns about Kenyan police. Questions whether the mission genuinely empowers Haitian self-governance.

Supportive Perspective
"Ruto Hails Success of Kenya-Led Security Mission in Haiti"
The Star (Kenya) · 2025
Read article on the-star.co.ke ↗

Reports restored freedom of movement, reopened roads, and successful national exams. Cites President Ruto praising the mission as proof R2P can work through Global South leadership.

5,500
Maximum uniformed personnel authorized for the Gang Suppression Force (UNSC Resolution 2793, October 2025) — the latest iteration of international security intervention in Haiti.
👥 Key Actors

States & IGOs

  • Haiti — Transitional government; limited sovereignty
  • Kenya — Led MSS; withdrew after GSF transition
  • UNSC — Authorized both MSS and GSF
  • "Core Group" — US, EU, Canada, France (critics say they steer Haiti policy)

Non-State Actors

  • Armed gangs — Territorial control of Port-au-Prince
  • Haitian National Police — Under-resourced, collaborating with GSF
  • Human rights orgs — HRW raised vetting and immunity concerns about MSS
🔍 Theory Lenses

🦁 Realism

Great powers use R2P rhetoric to advance their strategic interests. The "Core Group" controls Haiti's political direction — sovereignty is a fiction for weak states.

🕊 Liberalism

R2P is the liberal order's promise to prevent atrocities. Haiti tests whether that promise has substance — the gap between R2P rhetoric and actual resource commitment is damning.

🧠 Constructivism

The framing of Haiti as a "failed state" constructs it as permanently needing external management, denying Haitian agency and reproducing colonial narratives of incapacity.

📝 Exam Angle

Paper 2 Prompt

"To what extent is the Responsibility to Protect a tool of neocolonialism?" Haiti offers the strongest counter-case: real atrocities demand response, but the response mechanism bypasses local sovereignty.

Paper 3 Connection

Q2b Policy: Recommend the UNSC embed a mandatory Haitian civil society consultation mechanism within GSF operational planning. Q3 Essay: Link Security + Equality — how security interventions can deepen structural inequality if they lack local legitimacy.

Central Asia · Transboundary Resources

💧 Syr Darya: Water Protocols

Trilateral water-sharing between Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Kyrgyzstan
🌍 Environment 🛡 Security 🏚 Poverty
📋 Context

The Syr Darya — Central Asia's second-longest river — flows through Kyrgyzstan, Uzbekistan, and Kazakhstan, supplying irrigation for millions of farmers and hydroelectric power. In May 2025, the three states signed a trilateral protocol in Tashkent on water allocations and energy facility operations. The ICWC (Interstate Coordination Water Management Commission) set non-growing season allocations at 4.219 billion cubic meters for 2025-26, with Uzbekistan receiving the lion's share (3.347 billion m³). In 2026, Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan began installing joint automated hydro posts along the river, supported by Germany's GIZ, to improve transparency in water accounting.

4.2B m³

👥 Key Actors

States

  • Kyrgyzstan — Upstream; controls headwaters + Kambarata dam
  • Uzbekistan — Midstream; largest water consumer for agriculture
  • Kazakhstan — Downstream; vulnerable to reduced flow

IGOs & Partners

  • ICWC — Interstate commission managing allocations since 1992
  • GIZ (Germany) — Technical support for hydro monitoring
  • EABR — Eurasian Development Bank funding infrastructure
🔍 Theory Lenses

🦁 Realism

Upstream states (Kyrgyzstan) hold hydro-hegemonic power. Cooperation is strategic: Kyrgyzstan trades water for energy cooperation and dam funding. It's power balancing, not altruism.

🕊 Liberalism

The ICWC represents institutional cooperation — pooling sovereignty over a shared resource. The joint hydro posts show how transparency mechanisms can build trust.

🧠 Constructivism

Water is constructed as either a "shared river" or a "national resource" — the cooperative framing through the ICWC shapes state identity as partners rather than competitors.

📝 Exam Angle

Paper 2 Prompt

"Evaluate the effectiveness of international institutions in managing transboundary environmental challenges." The ICWC shows both success (continued cooperation since 1992) and limitation (climate change outpacing allocation models).

Paper 3 Connection

Q2b Policy: Recommend the ICWC adopt a climate-adjusted allocation model that accounts for glacial melt projections from IPCC data, with GIZ monitoring support. Q3 Essay: Link Environment + Security — how water scarcity can escalate from resource competition to security threat.
