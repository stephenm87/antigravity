The Big PictureWhat is Paper 3?

Paper 3 is a stimulus-based examination unique to HL students. You will receive one source (a text, data set, or image) related to a global political issue. You then answer four compulsory questions that test your ability to analyze the stimulus, connect it to your own researched case studies, propose a policy recommendation, and write an extended essay linking multiple Global Political Challenges (GPCs).

Key Difference from Paper 1 & Paper 2

Paper 3 is the only exam where you choose which GPC to apply to the stimulus. The stimulus is neutral — it's your job to connect it to one or more of the eight global political challenges you've studied. This means your case study knowledge is your greatest asset.

Question BreakdownThe Four Questions

Each question card includes detailed strategy and command terms. Click to collapse or expand.

3 marks

Analyze the Stimulus

Demonstrate understanding of the source material and link it to global political concepts.

🎯 Source reading + GPC identification

Command terms: Distinguish, Explain, Analyse, Suggest

Strategy: Read the stimulus twice. First pass: identify the topic, actors, and scale (local/national/global). Second pass: underline specific claims or data points you'll reference. Write 2-3 concise paragraphs linking your observations to a relevant GPC.

Time target: ~12 minutes

4 marks

Your Case Study

Identify a political issue from one of your researched case studies, referencing actors, causes, and impacts.

🔍 Case study recall + structural analysis

What they want: A specific, detailed case study that YOU have researched. Name the country/event, the key actors (state, IGO, NGO, MNC), the root cause of the issue, and its real-world impacts.

Strategy: Before the exam, prepare 3-4 case studies that span different GPCs. In the exam, choose the one that best maps onto the stimulus theme. Use the formula: Issue → Actor → Cause → Impact.

Time target: ~15 minutes

6 marks

Policy Recommendation

Propose a solution to the political issue from Q2a, supported by evidence and reasoned justification.

🏛 Actor + Mechanism + Rationale + Risk

This is the hardest question. You must move from analysis to prescription — what should be done?

The Formula:

  • 👤 Actor — Who implements? (e.g., UNSC, World Bank, regional body)
  • ⚙️ Mechanism — What tool? (sanction, treaty, fund, deployment)
  • 🎯 Rationale — Why this approach? Link to IR theory
  • ⚠️ Risk + Mitigation — What could go wrong? How to hedge?

Time target: ~20 minutes

15 marks

Extended Essay

Synthesize and evaluate, drawing connections across 2+ GPCs with reference to your researched case studies.

📝 Synthesis + Evaluation + Interconnectedness

This is worth over half the paper. It's a "to what extent" essay that demands you show how global political challenges are interconnected.

Structure:

  • 📌 Intro: Concept definition → Thesis → Case study preview
  • 🔨 Body 1: GPC #1 analysis with case study evidence
  • 🔨 Body 2: GPC #2 analysis — show the interconnection
  • ⚖️ Evaluation: "To what extent" — weigh the arguments
  • 🎯 Conclusion: Nuanced judgement, not a simple yes/no

Time target: ~40 minutes

The Golden Rule

Roughly 1 minute per mark, plus reading time. Q3 gets extra time because it requires planning, writing, and reviewing a full essay. Don't spend more than 15 minutes on Q2a — save your energy for Q2b and Q3.

Stimulus LiteracyReading the Stimulus

The 3-Pass Method

  1. Skim (30 sec): What is the topic? Who wrote it? What type of source is it (news article, data, report, speech)? Is there a date or geographic focus?
  2. Annotate (2 min): Underline key claims, circle actors (states, IGOs, NGOs), highlight data points. In the margin, write which GPC(s) connect to this stimulus.
  3. Connect (30 sec): Before writing, decide: which of MY case studies best maps onto this stimulus? Write the case study name at the top of your answer sheet.
🔗

The Bridge Sentence

The best Paper 3 answers start Q2a with a bridge sentence that links the stimulus to the student's own case study: "The dynamics described in the stimulus — [brief summary] — are mirrored in the case of [your case study], where..."

6
Examiner IntelligenceDo's & Don'ts

✅ Do This

  • Use the stimulus explicitly — quote or paraphrase specific lines
  • Name your case study in Q2a within the first sentence
  • In Q2b, identify a specific actor (not "the international community")
  • In Q3, show how GPCs interconnect — don't treat them in isolation
  • Use evaluative language: "to a significant extent," "while it is true that"
  • Link your analysis to at least one IR theory (Realism, Liberalism, Constructivism)

❌ Don't Do This

  • Ignore the stimulus and jump straight to your case study
  • Write a generic policy like "the UN should help" without mechanism detail
  • Treat Q3 as two separate mini-essays — it must be one integrated argument
  • List case studies without analysis ("China did X, Brazil did Y")
  • Forget to answer "to what extent" — you need a judgement, not just description
  • Spend 30+ minutes on Q1/Q2a and rush Q3 (it's worth 15 marks!)
🚨

The #1 Examiner Complaint

"Students describe rather than evaluate." Description tells the examiner what happened. Evaluation tells them why it matters, how significant it is, and what the implications are. Every body paragraph should end with a "So what?" sentence that makes a judgement.
