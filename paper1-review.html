Question 2 · 4 Marks · AO2Source + Own Knowledge: What the Examiner Wants

Q2 is worth 4 marks and tests AO2: Application and Analysis. Q2 typically asks you to analyze a source using both the source material and your own knowledge. This means going beyond describing what the source says — you need to explain why or how the political concepts in the source operate, supported by examples from your own study.

  • Identify a specific concept or claim from the source
  • Explain its significance using political concepts from the course
  • Support your analysis with a real-world example from your own knowledge
  • Explicitly refer to the source (quote or clear reference)

0 marks

Response does not reach the standard described by the descriptors, or is completely irrelevant.

1–2 marks

The response is primarily descriptive — it summarizes the source but lacks sufficient application of political concepts or examples from wider study.

3–4 marks

The response shows a clear understanding of the command term, provides accurate analysis, integrates relevant course knowledge/examples, and uses the source material effectively.

🎯

The Two-Paragraph Structure

A reliable structure for Q2:

  • Paragraph 1: Identify and explain a specific concept or claim found in the source, using direct references
  • Paragraph 2: Provide a relevant example or application from your own knowledge to deepen the analysis
⚠️

Common Q2 Mistake: The Summary Trap

Many students simply restate what the source says in their own words. This is description, not analysis. The examiner wants you to explain why the source matters using political concepts and your own knowledge. Ask yourself: "Am I explaining why this is significant, or am I just repeating what's already written?"

Question 3 · 6 Marks · AO3Compare and/or Contrast: What the Examiner Wants

Q3 is worth 6 marks and tests AO3: Synthesis and Evaluation. You must compare and/or contrast the views, ideas, claims, or information presented in two of the sources. The command term may ask you to compare (similarities), contrast (differences), or both — read the question carefully. Either way, you must actively integrate the sources throughout.

0 marks

Response does not reach the standard described by the descriptors, or is completely irrelevant.

1–2 marks · Limited

Sources are largely described rather than analyzed. Little or no attempt to compare or contrast. Response may treat sources separately rather than integrating them.

3–4 marks · Good

There is a clear attempt to compare or contrast, though it may lack depth or consistent "running" integration. References to sources are used, but analysis may be uneven or imbalanced.

5–6 marks · Excellent

A detailed, integrated comparison and contrast. Evidence is precise and well-selected. High analytical skill with appropriate political terminology. Well-structured and balanced between both sources.

🔑

The "Running Comparison" Rule

The biggest difference between Band 1–2 and Band 5–6 is integration. Do NOT write "Source A says X. Source B says Y." Instead, write: "While Source A argues X, Source B offers a contrasting perspective by suggesting Y, which reveals a fundamental disagreement about..." Every paragraph should reference both sources.

Use the PALETOT framework you've been practising to systematically identify comparison points across multiple dimensions — don't just compare content.

📏

Aim for 3–4 Distinct Points

High-scoring responses develop at least 3–4 substantive points of comparison or contrast. Each point should: (1) state the similarity or difference, (2) cite specific evidence from both sources, and (3) briefly explain the significance using political terminology.

The PALETOT Framework — Your Comparison Engine

Use PALETOT to systematically find similarities and differences between sources. Don't just compare what the sources say — examine the nature of the sources themselves:

Purpose
Why was this source created? To inform? To persuade? To justify a policy?
Actors
Which individuals, organizations, or states are named or implied?
Level of analysis
Global, international, regional, national, local, or community level?
Events
What specific events, data, or developments are referenced?
Tone
Optimistic or pessimistic? Neutral or advocacy? Urgent or measured?
Origin
Where does this source come from? An IGO? A state? An academic? An NGO?
Themes
Economic, political, social, cultural, or environmental themes?
💡

Linking Language Toolkit

Use these connectives to keep your comparison integrated:

  • Similarity: "Both sources agree that…" "Similarly, Source B also suggests…" "This perspective is echoed in Source C, which…"
  • Difference: "While Source A argues…, Source B presents a contrasting view by…" "In contrast to Source A's emphasis on…, Source B focuses on…" "Source A and Source B diverge fundamentally on…"

✅ Do This

  • Use a "running comparison" — both sources in every paragraph
  • Use PALETOT to find points across different dimensions (not just content)
  • Quote or paraphrase specific evidence from each source
  • Develop at least 3 distinct comparison points
  • Use political terminology naturally (e.g., sovereignty, legitimacy, power)
  • Balance your attention between both sources equally
  • Pay attention to the command term: compare, contrast, or both

❌ Don't Do This

  • Describe Source A in one block and then Source B in another (the "split" error)
  • Simply summarize what each source says without analyzing
  • Only compare on one PALETOT dimension (e.g., only content) — use multiple lenses
  • Bring in too much outside knowledge — Q3 is primarily about the sources
  • Make only 1–2 comparison points when 3–4 are needed for top band
  • Ignore one source or spend 80% on one and 20% on the other
  • Confuse "compare" (similarities) with "contrast" (differences) — read the command term

Apply Your SkillsPractice Sources with Exemplars
Source A: Adapted from UN Development Programme Report (2024)

Globalization and Inequality: A Double-Edged Sword

Globalization has lifted millions out of extreme poverty, with the World Bank reporting that the global extreme poverty rate fell from 36% in 1990 to under 9% by 2024. International trade agreements, foreign direct investment, and technological diffusion have accelerated economic development in countries like China, India, and Vietnam.

However, the benefits of globalization have not been evenly distributed. Within both developed and developing nations, income inequality has widened. The richest 1% of the global population now holds more wealth than the bottom 50% combined. The UN Special Rapporteur on Extreme Poverty has warned that "globalization without governance is a recipe for deepening inequality," arguing that international institutions have prioritized trade liberalization over social protection.

"We cannot celebrate a system that generates wealth while systematically concentrating it in the hands of the few. Development that excludes is not development — it is extraction." Philip Alston, Former UN Special Rapporteur on Extreme Poverty (2024)
Source B: Adapted from World Economic Forum and The Economist (2024)

The Case for Continued Global Integration

While critics emphasize inequality, proponents of globalization argue that the evidence overwhelmingly supports continued integration. Economic openness has been the single greatest driver of poverty reduction in human history. Countries that embraced trade — such as South Korea, Botswana, and Chile — have seen sustained improvements in HDI scores, life expectancy, and education access.

The WEF's 2024 Global Competitiveness Report argues that the solution to globalization's downsides is not less globalization, but better governance. Targeted social safety nets, progressive taxation, and investment in education can ensure that the gains from trade are more equitably distributed. Retreating into protectionism, the report warns, would harm the very populations that critics seek to protect, as developing nations depend on access to global markets for export revenue and employment.

"The answer to the challenges of globalization is not to retreat behind borders, but to build stronger institutions that distribute its benefits more fairly." World Economic Forum, Global Competitiveness Report (2024)

Q2 Exemplars: Source + Own Knowledge

"With reference to Source A and your own knowledge, explain why the UN Special Rapporteur argues that 'globalization without governance is a recipe for deepening inequality.'"

2/4

Medium Response

2 out of 4 marks · Mostly descriptive
📝 Student Response

The UN Special Rapporteur says that globalization without governance causes more inequality. This means that when countries trade with each other without proper rules, the rich get richer and the poor stay poor. ✓ Identifies the claim

Source A shows that the richest 1% have more wealth than the bottom 50%. This proves that globalization is making inequality worse. ✓ References source evidence

So the Special Rapporteur thinks we need more governance to fix this problem. ✗ No application of political concept ✗ No real-world example from own knowledge

⚖️ Examiner Verdict: 2/4

Why it lost marks:

  • Summarizes rather than analyzes. The student restates the source's argument without explaining why governance matters in political terms
  • No political concept applied. Words like "power," "interdependence," or "sovereignty" never appear. The examiner needs to see you connecting the source to course concepts
  • No example from own knowledge. A real-world case (e.g., WTO trade rules, NAFTA impacts) would demonstrate deeper understanding
4/4

High Response

4 out of 4 marks · Clear analysis with application
📝 Student Response

The UN Special Rapporteur argues that globalization without governance deepens inequality because the current international system prioritizes trade liberalization over social protection, meaning that the economic benefits of global integration flow disproportionately to those who already possess power — wealthy states, multinational corporations, and financial elites. ✓ Identifies the claim and begins to explain the mechanism

Source A illustrates this by noting that the richest 1% now hold more wealth than the bottom 50%, and that institutions have favored trade rules that benefit powerful economic actors. This reflects a deeper structural problem: globalization increases interdependence, but the governance structures that manage this interdependence — such as the WTO and IMF — are shaped by the interests of dominant states. Without reform, these institutions reproduce rather than reduce inequality. For example, IMF structural adjustment programs in Sub-Saharan Africa during the 1990s required governments to cut social spending in exchange for loans, which deepened poverty even as trade volumes grew — precisely the "extraction" that Alston describes. ✓ Applies concepts: power, interdependence, inequality ✓ Real-world example from own knowledge ✓ Links back to the source

✅ Examiner Verdict: 4/4

Why this earns full marks:

  • Analysis, not description. The student explains why governance matters (power imbalances in institutions) rather than just repeating what the source says
  • Applies political concepts: power, interdependence, inequality, governance — all used naturally
  • Own knowledge example: IMF structural adjustment in Sub-Saharan Africa is from the student's course study, not from the source
  • Links back to source: Connects the example to Alston's "extraction" quote

Q3 Exemplars: Compare and/or Contrast

"Compare and contrast the views expressed in Source A and Source B regarding the impact of globalization."

3/6

Medium Response

3 out of 6 marks · Attempt at comparison but lacks integration
📝 Student Response

Source A focuses on the negative impacts of globalization. It says that inequality has gotten worse and that the richest 1% hold more wealth than the bottom 50%. The Special Rapporteur calls this system "extraction" rather than development. ✓ Describes Source A

Source B has a more positive view. It says that globalization has reduced poverty and that countries like South Korea and Chile have benefited from trade. The WEF argues that the solution is better governance, not less globalization. ✓ Describes Source B

Both sources agree that governance is important for making globalization work better. However, Source A is more critical while Source B is more optimistic. ✓ Basic comparison point ✗ Sources treated separately — lacks "running" integration ✗ Only 1 genuine comparison point

⚖️ Examiner Verdict: 3/6 (Band 3–4)

Why this scored Band 3–4:

  • Sources treated separately. The first two paragraphs describe each source in isolation — this is the classic "split" error
  • Only one genuine comparison point (both agree governance matters). Band 5–6 requires 3–4 integrated points
  • Shallow analysis. "Source A is more critical while Source B is more optimistic" is surface-level. Why do they differ? What assumptions underpin each position?
6/6

High Response

6 out of 6 marks · Detailed, integrated comparison
📝 Student Response

Point 1 — Purpose & Tone (PALETOT: P, T): Source A and Source B have fundamentally different purposes and tones. Source A characterizes globalization as a system that "systematically concentrates" wealth — its purpose is critical advocacy, using an urgent, pessimistic tone to frame globalization as inherently producing inequality. Source B, by contrast, views it as "the single greatest driver of poverty reduction in human history" — its purpose is defence of the status quo, adopting an optimistic, solutions-oriented tone that emphasizes development. This reveals a core tension in global politics: whether globalization is a structural cause of inequality or its most effective remedy. ✓ Integrated comparison using PALETOT dimensions (Purpose, Tone)

Point 2 — Events & Evidence (PALETOT: E): Both sources use quantitative evidence, but they select data that supports opposing conclusions. Source A cites the wealth disparity between the top 1% and bottom 50% to highlight inequality within the global system, while Source B points to falling extreme poverty rates and successful development cases (South Korea, Chile) to emphasize inequality between nations declining. This contrast reveals how the same phenomenon — globalization — can be evaluated differently depending on whether one measures inequality within societies or between them. ✓ Nuanced comparison of evidence selection strategies

Point 3 — Themes: The role of governance (PALETOT: T): Perhaps the most striking similarity between the sources is their shared theme: that governance is essential. Source A's Rapporteur argues that "globalization without governance" deepens inequality, while Source B's WEF report calls for "stronger institutions." However, they diverge on what governance means: Source A implies that current institutions (like the WTO and IMF) are part of the problem, serving the interests of powerful states, while Source B suggests that these same institutions need reform, not replacement — framing governance as a mechanism to distribute the benefits of a fundamentally positive process. ✓ Identifies thematic similarity then reveals the deeper difference

Point 4 — Origin & Level of analysis (PALETOT: O, L): The sources originate from different institutional positions: Source A from a UN human rights watchdog (advocacy) and Source B from a private-sector forum (industry). This shapes their level of analysis — Source A operates at the global-systemic level, aligning with dependency theory (the structure itself is the problem), while Source B operates at the state-policy level, reflecting modernization theory (individual states can succeed through integration). This theoretical divergence explains why Source A sees globalization as "extraction" while Source B sees it as opportunity. ✓ Uses PALETOT's Origin and Level dimensions to anchor theoretical analysis

✅ Examiner Verdict: 6/6 (Band 5–6)

Why this earns top band:

  • Running comparison throughout: every paragraph mentions both sources and actively integrates them
  • 4 distinct comparison points using multiple PALETOT dimensions (Purpose, Tone, Events, Themes, Origin, Level) — not just content
  • High analytical skill: doesn't just say "A disagrees with B" but explains why they disagree and what assumptions drive each position
  • Appropriate political terminology: inequality, development, power, dependency theory, modernization theory — all used naturally and correctly
  • Balanced: equal attention to both sources in every paragraph
🔑

The Difference Between 3/6 and 6/6

The medium response describes each source separately then adds a brief comparison at the end. The high response integrates both sources in every paragraph using different PALETOT dimensions (Purpose, Tone, Events, Themes, Origin, Level) as the basis for each comparison point. Notice it also identifies where sources surprisingly agree (governance is needed) before revealing the deeper disagreement beneath that agreement. This layered, multi-dimensional analysis is what separates Band 3–4 from Band 5–6.

The FoundationThe 16 Key Concepts

The IB Global Politics course is built around 16 key concepts organized into four clusters. These are the analytical lenses you apply to every source, case study, and exam question. Click any card to expand its full definition.

🏛 Cluster 1: Power & Governance

The four "pillar" concepts that frame all analysis in the course.

⚡

Power

The ability to effect change. An aspect of relations among people within social organizations — not a unitary force, but contested relationships.
Power is a central concept in the study of global politics and a key focus of the course. Power can be seen as ability to effect change and, rather than being viewed as a unitary or independent force, is as an aspect of relations among people functioning within a social organization. Contested relationships between people and groups of people dominate politics, particularly in this era of increased globalization, and so understanding the dynamics of power plays a prominent role in understanding global politics.

Core question: "Who has the ability to make things happen?"
Pillar Concept
🏴

Sovereignty

A state's independence, its control over territory, and its ability to govern itself without external interference.
Sovereignty characterizes a state's independence, its control over territory and its ability to govern itself. How states use their sovereign power is at the heart of many important issues in global politics. Some theorists argue that sovereign power is increasingly being eroded by aspects of globalization such as global communication and trade, which states cannot always fully control. Others argue that sovereign states exercise a great deal of power when acting in their national interest and that this is unlikely to change.

Core question: "Who has the legal right to rule this territory?"
Pillar Concept
✅

Legitimacy

An actor or action being commonly considered acceptable — the fundamental basis for governance and exercising power over others.
Legitimacy refers to an actor or an action being commonly considered acceptable and provides the fundamental basis or rationale for all forms of governance and other ways of exercising power over others. The most accepted contemporary source of legitimacy in a state is some form of democracy or constitutionalism whereby the governed have a defined and periodical opportunity to choose who they wish to exercise power over them. Other sources of legitimacy are suggested in states in which such an opportunity does not exist. Within any proposed overall framework of legitimacy, individual actions by a state can be considered more or less legitimate.

Core question: "Why should we obey this authority?"
Pillar Concept
🔗

Interdependence

Mutual reliance between groups, organizations, and states for access to resources that sustain living arrangements.
In global politics, the concept of interdependence most often refers to the mutual reliance between and among groups, organizations, geographic areas and/or states for access to resources that sustain living arrangements. Often, this mutual reliance is economic (such as trade), but can also have a security dimension (such as defence arrangements) and, increasingly, a sustainability dimension (such as environmental treaties). Globalization has increased interdependence, while often changing the relationships of power among the various actors engaged in global politics.

Core question: "How do actors depend on each other?"
Pillar Concept

⚖️ Cluster 2: Rights & Justice

Concepts that deal with what individuals and groups are owed.

📜

Human Rights

Basic claims that one should be able to exercise simply by virtue of being a human being — many argue they are inalienable and universal.
Human rights are basic claims and entitlements that, many argue, one should be able to exercise simply by virtue of being a human being. Many contemporary thinkers argue they are essential for living a life of dignity, are inalienable, and should be accepted as universal. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights adopted by the UN in 1948 is recognized as the beginning of the formal discussion of human rights around the world. Critics argue that human rights are a Western, or at least culturally relative, concept.
Rights & Justice
⚖️

Justice

Closely associated with fairness and with individuals getting what they deserve — though what "deserve" means is contested.
There are a number of different interpretations of the concept of justice. It is often closely associated with the idea of fairness and with individuals getting what they deserve, although what is meant by deserve is also contested. One avenue is to approach justice through the idea of rights, and what individuals can legitimately expect of one another or of their government. Some theorists also argue that equality not only in the institutions and procedures of a society but also in capabilities or well-being outcomes is required for justice to be realized.
Rights & Justice
🗽

Liberty

Having freedom and autonomy — divided into positive liberty (freedom to) and negative liberty (freedom from).
The concept of liberty refers to having freedom and autonomy. It is often divided into positive and negative liberty, with negative liberty defined as individuals having the freedom from external coercion and positive liberty defined as individuals having the autonomy to carry out their own rational will. Some scholars reject this distinction and argue that in practice, one form of liberty cannot exist without the other. It is also questioned if such an understanding of liberty is sufficient for an interdependent world, in which the seeming freedom and autonomy of some may depend on lack of some forms of liberty for others.
Rights & Justice
⚖

Equality

The idea that all people have the same intrinsic value — linked to justice and, increasingly, to liberty.
Egalitarian theories are based on a concept of equality that all people, or groups of people, are seen as having the same intrinsic value. Equality is therefore closely linked to justice and fairness, as egalitarians argue that justice can only exist if there is equality. Increasingly, with growing polarization within societies, equality is also linked to liberty, as different people have differing possibilities to be free and autonomous. Hence, debates on equality inform our understanding of liberty as well.
Rights & Justice

☮️ Cluster 3: Peace & Conflict

Concepts that deal with the dynamics of how political actors interact.

☮️

Peace

Both the absence of conflict/violence AND a state of harmonious relations. The ultimate goal of many organizations.
Peace is often defined as both the absence of conflict and violence as well as a state of harmonious relations. Many also refer to peace as a personal state of non-conflict, particularly with oneself and with one's relationship to others. Peace is the ultimate goal of many organizations that monitor and regulate social relationships.
Peace & Conflict
💥

Conflict

The dynamic process of actual or perceived opposition between individuals or groups over positions, interests, or values.
Conflict is the dynamic process of actual or perceived opposition between individuals or groups. This could be opposition over positions, interests or values. Most theorists would distinguish between non-violent and violent conflict. In this distinction, non-violent conflict can be a useful mechanism for social change and transformation, while violent conflict is harmful and requires conflict resolution.
Peace & Conflict
🔥

Violence

Physical or psychological force afflicted upon another being. Broadly: anything preventing others from reaching their full potential.
Violence is often defined as physical or psychological force afflicted upon another being. In the context of global politics, it could be seen as anything someone does that prevents others from reaching their full potential. This broader definition would encompass unequal distribution of power that excludes entire groups from accessing resources essential for improved living standards or well-being, and discriminatory practices that exclude entire groups of people from accessing certain resources.
Peace & Conflict
✊

Non-violence

Advocating for rights without physically harming the opponent — through boycotts, demonstrations, and civil disobedience.
Non-violence is the practice of advocating one's own or others' rights without physically harming the opponent. It often involves actively opposing the system that is deemed to be unjust, through for example boycotts, demonstrations and civil disobedience. Theorists argue that non-violence can often draw attention to a conflict situation and that it could provide a fertile basis for post-conflict transformation.
Peace & Conflict

🌍 Cluster 4: Development & Sustainability

Concepts that deal with how societies grow and distribute resources.

📈

Development

Sustained increase in living standards and well-being — beyond just income to include education, health, and institutions.
Development is a sustained increase in the standard of living and well-being of a level of social organization. Many consider it to involve increased income; better access to basic goods and services; improvements in education, health care and public health; well-functioning institutions; decreased inequality; reduced poverty and unemployment; and more sustainable production and consumption patterns. The focus in contemporary global politics is on issues faced by developing countries, and on the imperative of shifting the focus from modernization (seen as Westernization).
Development
🌱

Sustainability

Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs.
Definitions of sustainability begin with the idea that development should meet the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs. Sustainability today has three fields of debate — environmental, sociopolitical and economic. In global politics, mechanisms and incentives required for political institutions, economic actors and individuals to take a longer term and more inclusive well-being perspective in their decision-making are particularly important.
Development
📊

Inequality

A state where equality between people or groups is not realized, manifesting through unequal access to resources.
Inequality refers to a state of affairs where equality between people or groups of people is not realized and the consequent potential compromises of justice and liberty. Inequality often manifests itself through unequal access to resources that are needed to sustain life and develop individuals and communities. Consequently, the concept is closely connected to discussions of power and of who holds the rights to these resources. Inequality can be examined both as a phenomenon within and between societies.
Development
🌐

Globalization

The process by which the world's economies, societies, and cultures become increasingly integrated and connected.
Globalization is a process by which the world's local, national and regional economies, societies and cultures are becoming increasingly integrated and connected. The term refers to the reduction of barriers and borders, as people, goods, services and ideas flow more freely between different parts of the world. Globalization has been taking place for centuries but the pace has quickened in recent decades, facilitated by developments in transportation and communication technology, and powered by cheap energy. It is now widely acknowledged that globalization has both benefits and drawbacks and that its benefits are not evenly distributed.
Development

How They ConnectKey Concept Relationships

In the exam, you are expected to use concepts as analytical lenses, not just define them. Understanding how concepts relate to each other is what separates a Band 1–2 answer ("Power is the ability to effect change") from a Band 5–6 answer ("The tension between sovereignty and interdependence created a legitimacy crisis for..."). Here are the critical relationships:

⚡ Power ↔ 🏴 Sovereignty Core Tension

Sovereignty is often viewed as a "bundle" of powers. A state exercises its sovereignty by using power (military, economic, diplomatic) to control territory and interact with others. However, globalization has created a fundamental tension: states may retain legal sovereignty while losing actual power to control outcomes — as transnational corporations, international organizations, and global market forces operate beyond any single state's control. Ask: does this state have sovereignty in name but not in practice?

⚡ Power ↔ ✅ Legitimacy Stability Link

Power can be exercised through coercion (force), but it is far more stable and effective when viewed as legitimate. A government with power but no legitimacy must rely on brute force, which is fragile. Conversely, legitimacy without power is hollow — a government accepted by its people but unable to deliver services loses legitimacy over time. Ask: does this actor's power rest on legitimate authority or coercion?

🏴 Sovereignty ↔ ✅ Legitimacy Recognition

A state's claim to sovereignty often depends on its legitimacy — both from its own citizens (internal legitimacy) and from the international community (external recognition). A newly formed state may control its borders (sovereignty) but struggle without international recognition (legitimacy). The R2P doctrine directly challenges this link: it argues that sovereignty is conditional on the state's legitimate protection of its citizens.

🏴 Sovereignty ↔ 🔗 Interdependence Key Debate

This is one of the central debates in the course. Interdependence requires states to cooperate — trade agreements, environmental treaties, security alliances — which inevitably limits sovereignty. States must balance the benefits of cooperation against the cost of ceding control. Ask: did this state sacrifice sovereignty for the benefits of interdependence, or did it prioritize sovereignty at the cost of isolation?

⚖️ Justice ↔ ⚖ Equality Mutually Dependent

Egalitarians argue that justice can only exist where there is equality. If people do not have equal access to rights, resources, and opportunities, then "fairness" is an illusion. However, some theorists argue that justice requires treating people differently based on their needs (equity vs. equality). Ask: does this policy promote equal treatment or equitable outcomes?

📈 Development ↔ 🌱 Sustainability Tension

Development historically focused on economic growth, but sustainability challenges this by adding environmental and intergenerational dimensions. The tension: can a developing nation industrialize rapidly (development) without destroying its environment (sustainability)? The SDGs attempt to reconcile this by integrating economic, social, and environmental goals.

🌐 Globalization ↔ 📊 Inequality Contested

Proponents argue globalization reduces inequality between nations (rising Global South). Critics argue it increases inequality within nations (wealth concentration). This dual effect means you must always specify: inequality between whom? Globalization's relationship with inequality depends entirely on the scale of analysis.

☮️ Peace ↔ 💥 Conflict ↔ ✊ Non-violence Spectrum

Peace is not simply the absence of conflict. Non-violent conflict can be a positive force for social change (e.g., civil rights movements, labor strikes). The IB distinguishes between negative peace (absence of violence) and positive peace (presence of justice, equality, and human rights). Non-violence operates at the intersection: it creates conflict without violence, aiming for positive peace.

📜 Human Rights ↔ 🏴 Sovereignty R2P Tension

When a state violates its citizens' human rights, should the international community intervene even if it means violating that state's sovereignty? This is the R2P (Responsibility to Protect) dilemma — and one of the most important tensions in the course. Ask: which takes precedence — the state's sovereignty or its citizens' human rights?

PowerThe capacity to act — can come from force, wealth, or influence.
vs
LegitimacyThe acceptance that authority is rightful — comes from consent, law, or tradition.
Memory trick: A dictator has power without legitimacy. A newly elected democratic government has legitimacy — but does it have the power to govern effectively?

Sovereignty vs. Legitimacy

SovereigntyAbout control — who governs this territory?
vs
LegitimacyAbout acceptance — do people/states accept this governance as rightful?
Memory trick: Taiwan exercises sovereignty (it governs itself) but lacks full international legitimacy (many states don't recognize it as a sovereign state).

Equality vs. Justice

EqualityAll people have the same intrinsic value and should have equal treatment/opportunity.
vs
JusticePeople get what they deserve — what's "fair," which might mean treating people differently based on need.
Memory trick: Equality = everyone gets the same-sized shoe. Justice/equity = everyone gets a shoe that fits. They overlap but are not identical.

Equality vs. Inequality

EqualityA normative ideal — what should be. A value judgment.
vs
InequalityA descriptive reality — what is. A measurable condition.
Memory trick: Equality is the goal; inequality is the problem. When using them in essays, be clear whether you're describing a value (equality) or a situation (inequality).

Peace vs. Non-violence

PeaceA state or condition — the absence of conflict/violence AND harmonious relations.
vs
Non-violenceA strategy or practice — actively opposing injustice without physical harm.
Memory trick: Non-violence is a method that may actually create conflict (protests, boycotts) in pursuit of a more just peace. Non-violence ≠ peace.

Conflict vs. Violence

ConflictOpposition between groups over positions, interests, or values. Can be non-violent and even constructive.
vs
ViolencePhysical or psychological force. Always harmful. Includes structural violence (systemic inequality).
Memory trick: All violence involves conflict, but not all conflict involves violence. A labor strike is conflict without violence. Conflict can be a force for positive change; violence cannot.

Development vs. Globalization

DevelopmentImproving quality of life within a society — income, health, education, institutions.
vs
GlobalizationThe process of integration between societies — trade, communication, cultural exchange.
Memory trick: Globalization is a process; development is an outcome. Globalization can promote or hinder development depending on governance and power dynamics.

Liberty vs. Human Rights

LibertyFreedom and autonomy — being free from coercion (negative) and free to act (positive).
vs
Human RightsSpecific entitlements — codified claims (e.g., right to life, education, expression).
Memory trick: Liberty is a broad principle; human rights are specific entitlements. Human rights protect specific liberties, but not all aspects of liberty are codified as human rights.

Sustainability vs. Development

SustainabilityMeeting present needs without compromising future generations. Long-term thinking.
vs
DevelopmentIncreasing living standards now. Often focuses on short- to medium-term improvements.
Memory trick: Development asks "How do we grow?" Sustainability asks "How do we grow without destroying the future?" They can conflict when rapid industrialization degrades the environment.

Interdependence vs. Globalization

InterdependenceA condition — actors depend on each other for resources, security, trade.
vs
GlobalizationA process — the increasing integration of economies, societies, and cultures.
Memory trick: Globalization is the driver; interdependence is the result. Globalization deepens interdependence, but interdependence can exist without globalization (e.g., neighboring states trading for centuries).
🚨

The Big Three Confusion: Power / Sovereignty / Legitimacy

Students most often confuse these three because in practice, they act together. A dictator may have power (army) but lack legitimacy (popular consent). A new state may be sovereign (controls borders) but lack legitimacy (international recognition). A powerful nation may use its power to violate another's sovereignty, sparking debate about the legitimacy of intervention.

In the exam: Always ask three separate questions: (1) Who has the ability to act? (Power) (2) Who has the right to govern? (Sovereignty) (3) Is this action/authority accepted? (Legitimacy)
