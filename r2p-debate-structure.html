F
The Format How the Fishbowl Works

Five of you will debate inside the inner circle (Pro vs. Con). Two of you will sit in the outer ring as active observers. The debate pauses at three structured moments so the observers can ask questions, challenge evidence, and ultimately deliver a verdict on which side argued more convincingly.

Observers are not passive. You have a defined role at every stage, and the debate literally stops for you. Your questions will push the debaters to explain better, and your final verdict gives the debate real stakes.

O
Chloe + Jasmine Your Objectives by Phase

Keep your notepad or device open the whole time. Your job is inquiry first, judgment last. Here's what to focus on at each stage:

1

During Opening Statements

Write down: "What is the core disagreement?" Capture each side's main claim in one sentence.

2

During Arguments

For each side, note the single strongest piece of evidence. Also write down anything you don't understand — that's your question for the next pause.

3

During Cross-Examination

Note which side handles tough questions better. Who answered directly? Who deflected? This feeds your final verdict.

4

Fishbowl Pause 3 — Your Verdict

State which side you find more convincing and cite 1–2 specific moments from the debate. Your reasoning matters more than your answer.

Chloe + Jasmine Question Stems to Use During Pauses

Use these as starting points during fishbowl pauses. You don't need to read them word-for-word — adapt them to what you actually heard.

Clarifying

"Can you explain what you mean by...?"

"When you say [R2P / sovereignty / intervention], what does that actually look like?"

"I'm not sure I follow — can you walk me through that?"

Probing Evidence

"How do you know that? Where does that come from?"

"Is that one example, or has it happened more than once?"

"What would the other side say about that evidence?"

Making Connections

"How does this connect to what the other team said about...?"

"Does this apply to other countries, or just this case?"

"If this is true, what does that mean for...?"

Constructive Challenge

"What would happen if the opposite were true?"

"What's the strongest argument against your position?"

"Are there any downsides to what you're proposing?"

First 10 Minutes Team Prep

Leah, Rachel, Matthew, Marcus, Claire — Debaters

  1. Confirm who speaks when (1 min) — use the roles above
  2. Share and rank your evidence (4 min) — pick the 2–3 strongest points
  3. Anticipate the other side (3 min) — what will they argue? Plan 2–3 counter-responses
  4. Plan your narrative arc (2 min) — lead with impact, build with evidence, close emotionally

Chloe and Jasmine — Observers

While the teams prep, you'll receive and read a 1-page R2P briefing that covers:

  • What R2P is (the 3 pillars, in 3 sentences)
  • Today's debate question
  • The case studies being debated (Venezuela and Iran)
  • Your question stem card (above) and task sheet

You don't need to take a side yet. Your job is to come in with fresh eyes, ask good questions, and form your opinion by the end based on what you hear.

Each of you asks 1–2 questions that dig into the evidence. Use "Probing Evidence" or "Constructive Challenge" stems.

Round 3: Cross-Examination

~2.5 min per side. Matthew and Claire take turns directing questions at the opposing team. Any team member can answer.

Matthew Claire

Round 4: Rebuttals

~2.5 min per side. Directly attack the other team's strongest arguments. Reference specific claims they made.

Leah Marcus

Round 5: Closing Statements

2 min per side. Leah closes for Pro, Marcus closes for Con. Summarize your case — no new evidence. Marcus gets the final word.

Each of you states which side you find more convincing and explains why, citing 1–2 specific moments.

Everyone

Debrief

Step out of your roles. Quick reflection:

  • Did anyone's thinking change?
S
For All Debaters Strategies That Win Debates

1. The "Steel Man" Rule

Before you rebut, restate the other side's argument in its strongest form. This proves you understood them — and makes your rebuttal more credible when you knock it down.

2. The Evidence Sandwich

Structure every point as: Claim → Evidence → So What? The "so what" links it back to R2P. If you can't explain why your evidence matters, don't use it.

3. Think About Chloe and Jasmine

They're hearing about R2P for the first time through your arguments. If they can't follow you, your argument isn't clear enough. Avoid jargon without explaining it. When you say "R2P" or "sovereignty," briefly define what you mean.

4. Pass Notes

When you're not speaking, write notes and pass them to your teammates who are about to speak. This keeps you engaged and makes your team's rebuttals stronger.
