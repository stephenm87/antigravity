Warm-Up · ~8 min Knowledge Check — 10 Questions
💡

How it works

Every question focuses purely on R2P doctrine and the theories that explain it. Select the best answer — instant feedback appears after you click. Aim for 8+.

Question 1
Which year and document formally established R2P as an international norm?
R2P was unanimously endorsed by 150+ heads of state at the 2005 UN World Summit. Crucially it is a political commitment — not a binding treaty — which is why enforcement remains contested.
Question 2
R2P is triggered only when mass atrocity crimes occur. Which of these is NOT one of the four trigger crimes?
R2P's four triggers are genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity. Nuclear proliferation is a state-security issue handled through the NPT — not an R2P trigger. This distinction will be critical when we analyse our upcoming case studies.
Question 3
Pillar I of R2P assigns the primary responsibility for protecting populations to…
Pillar I is the foundation: sovereignty now comes with a responsibility, not just a right. Every state must protect its own population from the four atrocity crimes. Failure to do so triggers Pillars II and III.
Question 4
Pillar II of R2P focuses on the role of the international community before a crisis escalates. Which example best illustrates Pillar II?
Pillar II is about international assistance and encouragement — helping states build capacity to protect their populations. Think of it as preventive: early warning systems, legal training, institutional support. Intervention (Pillar III) is a last resort.
Question 5
Pillar III allows the international community to act decisively. What structural feature makes Pillar III activation extremely difficult in practice?
Any one of the five permanent members (US, UK, France, Russia, China) can veto UNSC resolutions. When great-power interests diverge — as with Syria — the veto prevents Pillar III from activating even when atrocities are occurring.
Question 6
The Oxford Academic article you read describes R2P as experiencing a "false death." What does this phrase mean?
"False death" captures a Constructivist insight: even when states bypass R2P procedures, they still feel compelled to use humanitarian language — proving the norm hasn't collapsed, it is under stress. This is key for evaluating our upcoming cases.
Question 7
Which IR theory is most comfortable with R2P as a genuine international obligation?
Liberals believe international institutions (like the UNSC), international law, and shared norms can genuinely constrain state behaviour. R2P fits the Liberal project: multilateral, legalistic, and focused on individual rights over state sovereignty.
Question 8
A Realist critique of R2P would most likely argue that…
Realists are cynical: states act in self-interest in an anarchic system. R2P-style language can be used to legitimise power projection. When humanitarian missions happen to remove unfriendly regimes or secure resources, Realists see the real motive.
Question 9
A Constructivist would focus on which aspect of R2P?
Constructivists study how ideas, norms, and identity shape international behaviour. R2P is a prime case: a political commitment (not law) that has nonetheless changed how states talk about sovereignty — shifting from an absolute right to a conditional one.
Question 10
R2P redefines the concept of sovereignty. Which pairing correctly captures this shift?
This is the conceptual heart of R2P. Kofi Annan's "two sovereignties" framing and the ICISS Commission both argued that sovereignty cannot shield governments that slaughter their own people. R2P made protection of populations a condition of sovereign legitimacy.

The Responsibility to Protect remains a contested norm rather than an enforceable legal obligationCLAIM — the analytical point this paragraph will prove, undermined structurally by the P5 veto mechanism within the UN Security Council. From a Realist perspective, this outcome is entirely predictable: permanent members exercise their veto when intervention would conflict with strategic alliances or regional spheres of influence, confirming that R2P cannot override great-power interest in an anarchic systemTHEORY LENS — applying Realism to explain why the evidence occurred. However, a Constructivist would counter that the norm retains moral authority — even Russia invoked humanitarian language to justify its own Syria engagement, demonstrating that R2P has reshaped the terms of legitimate discourse even where enforcement failedEVALUATION — conceding the counterargument and weighing it honestly. On balance, R2P's structural dependence on UNSC consensus renders it highly effective as rhetoric and highly fragile as mechanism — a norm that constrains the language of international politics even when it cannot constrain its practice.
