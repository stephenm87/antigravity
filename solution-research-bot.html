The Process

2 Six Research Prompts

Work through these six questions for each case study to accumulate the precise information you need for a Band 7 recommendation.

Prompt 1 — Maps to: Problem Context

🔍 What's broken?

Describe the core problem in one sentence. A problem statement backed by a statistic, date, or named impact is far more powerful than a vague claim. Not "things are bad in Haiti" but a precise, evidence-driven statement.
"Armed gangs control approximately 80% of Port-au-Prince, with 5,600 people killed in gang violence in 2024 alone, while the Haitian National Police lacks the capacity to restore order."
✅ Self-Check
  • Does it name a specific location or population affected?
  • Does it include a statistic, percentage, or measurable impact?
  • Is the injustice or governance failure clearly named?
Prompt 2 — Maps to: ACTOR

🏛 Who has the power to fix it?

Name 2–3 specific actors who have the authority, resources, or jurisdiction to address this problem. For each, name the specific sub-body and the tool they control. Don't say "the UN" — say which organ, which mandate, which fund.
"1) UN Security Council (Chapter VII authority) — binding resolutions, peacekeeping mandates, sanctions
2) Peacebuilding Commission — institution-building funds, post-conflict governance support
3) ICRC — humanitarian access negotiation, protection mandates"
✅ Self-Check
  • Did you name the specific sub-body (not just the parent organization)?
  • Did you identify the specific tool or mandate each actor controls?
  • Is the actor actually authorized to act on this type of issue?
Prompt 3 — Maps to: MECHANISM

⚙️ What's the move?

For your strongest actor, describe ONE concrete, verifiable policy action. Ask: "Could an independent observer verify whether this was done?" If the answer is no, your mechanism is too vague. Use the templates in Section 4.
"The Security Council should pass a binding Chapter VII resolution mandating the Multinational Security Support mission to embed Haitian National Police officers in all operational units at a minimum 1:3 ratio, overseen by a community oversight board with quarterly reporting to the PBC."
✅ Self-Check
  • Is the action concrete (not "help" or "intervene" or "address")?
  • Does it follow a mechanism template (amend / certify / condition / establish)?
  • Could an observer verify whether this policy was implemented?
Prompt 4 — Maps to: RATIONALE

💡 Why would it actually work?

Provide 2–3 pieces of evidence that your mechanism is feasible. The strongest rationales combine: a precedent (it's worked before), an existing system (infrastructure already exists), and data showing urgency (why now).
"1) MINUSTAH (2004–2017) demonstrates that military effectiveness without local legitimacy collapses — its cholera epidemic killed 10,000+
2) The current MSS operates without a Chapter VII mandate, meaning no accountability mechanisms exist
3) 5,600 deaths in 2024 alone demonstrates the urgency of structured intervention"
✅ Self-Check
  • Do you cite a historical precedent (what worked or failed before)?
  • Do you reference an existing framework, treaty, or system?
  • Do you include data or evidence that supports urgency?
Prompt 5 — Maps to: RISK

⚠️ What could go wrong?

Name the single most credible obstacle. A strong risk names a specific actor or dynamic — not "it might not work" but "China's veto history suggests UNSC authorization would face P5 resistance." See Section 5 for categories.
"Armed gangs controlling approximately 80% of Port-au-Prince may exploit consultation timelines to consolidate territorial control during the oversight board transition period, undermining the legitimacy of the civilian oversight mechanism."
✅ Self-Check
  • Is the risk credible and specific to your case?
  • Does it name a specific actor or dynamic that would obstruct?
  • Can you categorize it (sovereignty / economic / enforcement / timeline)?
Prompt 6 — Maps to: Risk Mitigation (the "However..." Pivot)

🛡 How do you mitigate it?

Propose a specific countermeasure — ideally naming a secondary actor or alternative mechanism. This is the sentence that elevates Band 5–6 answers to Band 7: "However, this could be mitigated by..."
"The Security Council should authorize a phased approach: immediate security operations for 90 days under sole MSS command, followed by a gradual transfer to the community oversight board. The Peacebuilding Commission should pair this with a 5-year policing infrastructure fund ($50M minimum)."
✅ Self-Check
  • Does it name a secondary actor (different from the main actor)?
  • Is the mitigation mechanism specific and actionable?
  • Does it directly address the identified risk?

Templates

4 Mechanism Templates

Strong mechanisms follow one of these four patterns. Fill in the blanks with your specific case study details.

Amend an Existing Framework
[Actor] should amend [existing treaty/regulation] to require [specific new obligation] verified by [enforcement body].
Create Certification / Conditionality
[Actor] should require all [companies/states] to obtain a [certificate/audit] before accessing [market/funding].
Link Market Access to Compliance
[Actor] should condition [trade access/funding] on demonstrated compliance with [standard/protocol], with non-compliant parties facing [consequence].
Establish an Oversight Body
[Actor] should create a [board/commission] comprising [specific stakeholders], with the authority to [review/approve specific actions].
Obstacles

5 Risk Categories

Most policy risks fall into one of four categories. Identify which applies to your case and name the specific dynamic.

⚠️ Sovereignty Resistance

A state frames the intervention as external interference, undermining domestic political support or invoking non-intervention norms.

e.g., "China's consistent veto pattern on sovereignty grounds suggests UNSC authorization would face P5 resistance."

⚠️ Economic Disruption

The policy risks disrupting supply chains, raising consumer prices, or threatening livelihoods — creating opposition from economic stakeholders.

e.g., "Mandatory conflict mineral certification could raise smartphone component costs by 12%, incentivizing cheaper non-compliant alternatives."

⚠️ Enforcement Gap

The target state or actor may block auditors, expel NGOs, refuse compliance, or lack institutional capacity to implement.

e.g., "Myanmar's military junta expelled the UN Special Envoy in 2021, demonstrating its capacity to block international oversight."

⚠️ Timeline Conflict

Consultation processes, bureaucratic delays, or phased implementation may be too slow for an urgent crisis, allowing the situation to deteriorate.

e.g., "The 18-month treaty ratification timeline exceeds the WHO's projected peak infection window of 6 months."
Model Answers

6 Tiered Examples with Elevation Moves

See how the same recommendation improves from Band 3–4 to Band 7 — and understand exactly which moves create each jump.

Band 3–4 Generic and vague

"The United Nations should intervene in Haiti to help stop violence. They could send peacekeepers and provide aid. This would help because Haiti is very dangerous and people are suffering. The international community has a responsibility to protect civilians."

🔼 Moves That Elevate to Band 5–6

  • Replace "The United Nations" with a specific sub-body (e.g., "UN Security Council acting under Chapter VII")
  • Replace "intervene" with a verifiable mechanism (e.g., "pass a binding resolution mandating...")
  • Add a specific precedent or data point as rationale
  • Name the specific tool the actor controls (e.g., "binding resolutions, peacekeeping mandate authority")

Band 5–6 Specific actor and mechanism

"The UN Security Council should pass a binding Chapter VII resolution mandating the Multinational Security Support mission to embed Haitian National Police officers in all operational units at a minimum 1:3 ratio, ensuring local capacity-building alongside security operations. This is warranted because MINUSTAH (2004–2017) demonstrated that military operations without local police integration created dependency without sustainable security gains, and 5,600 gang-related deaths in 2024 demonstrate the urgency of restructured intervention."

🔼 Moves That Elevate to Band 7

  • Acknowledge a credible obstacle (risk) with a specific actor or dynamic
  • Add the "However... which could be mitigated by..." pivot
  • Name a secondary actor for the mitigation (e.g., Peacebuilding Commission)
  • Include a specific mechanism for the mitigation (e.g., "$50M funding commitment, quarterly reporting")

Band 7 Full AMR² with evidence, risk, and mitigation

"The UN Security Council should pass a binding Chapter VII resolution mandating the MSS to embed Haitian National Police officers in all operational units at a minimum 1:3 ratio, overseen by a community oversight board with quarterly reporting to the Peacebuilding Commission. This is feasible because MINUSTAH's failure to integrate local forces resulted in 10,000+ cholera deaths and zero sustainable security gains, while the current MSS operates without Chapter VII accountability. However, armed gangs controlling 80% of Port-au-Prince may exploit the oversight transition timeline to consolidate territorial control. This could be mitigated by authorizing an initial 90-day sole MSS security phase before civilian oversight transfer, paired with a $50 million PBC policing infrastructure fund to ensure the HNP has the capacity to sustain gains independently."
