How You're ScoredThe Q1 Rubric

Question 1 is worth 3 marks and uses a question-specific mark scheme developed for each paper. The stimulus is there to stimulate your thinking, not for source interpretation. Simply repeating the content of the stimulus will not earn high marks. You must demonstrate understanding of the terminology and concepts by drawing on your own knowledge of HL topic areas.

1

1 Identify

Name the relevant concept, issue, or GPC from the stimulus. Be specific and direct and don't bury it in a paragraph. Put it in your first sentence.

2

2 Demonstrate

Show your own understanding. Use the stimulus as a springboard to demonstrate knowledge from your HL studies. Reference specific elements in the stimulus as they relate to your political challenges and topic areas. Identify connections, causes, or implications that go beyond what the text says.

3

3 Explain

Demonstrate understanding by explaining the significance. Why does this matter? How does it connect to the broader issue? This is where you move from description to analysis.

💡

Stimulus ≠ Source Interpretation

The stimulus is there to stimulate your thinking. It is not a source interpretation task. Read it carefully, understand the context, then use your own knowledge to suggest (for example) causes, challenges, criticisms, benefits, or outcomes. You may also be asked to distinguish between different meanings of a terminology. The mark scheme is question-specific, so read the question carefully and follow its instructions.

Decode the QuestionCommand Terms

The Q1 command term tells you exactly what the examiner expects. Using the wrong approach (e.g., "distinguishing" when asked to "suggest") costs marks even if your knowledge is correct. Click each card to see what's expected.

🔍 Identify

Name or point out a specific element.

What they want: A clear, direct naming of the concept, actor, or issue. No explanation needed; just precision.

"The stimulus identifies a tension between state sovereignty and human rights enforcement."

↔️ Distinguish

Describe the differences between two or more items.

What they want: A clear comparison showing how two things are different. Use "whereas" or "in contrast" to signal comparison. Give an example for each side.

"The Global North refers to industrialized, wealthy nations such as the US, whereas the Global South refers to developing states such as Nigeria, often with colonial histories."

💬 Suggest

Propose possible answers, reasons, or solutions.

What they want: Your own inference, grounded in the stimulus. You're not just quoting; you're interpreting and drawing out implications. Each suggestion should be a distinct, separate point.

"One issue is that the label 'Global South' implies uniformity across states that have vastly different levels of development, masking internal inequalities."

📖 Explain

Give a detailed account including reasons and causes.

What they want: More than naming. You need to show how or why something works. Include a causal chain: "This happens because… which leads to…"

"Climate finance is contentious because wealthy nations historically responsible for emissions resist binding commitments, which perpetuates inequality as vulnerable states bear the costs of climate impacts they did not cause."

🔬 Analyse

Break down a concept to explore its components and relationships.

What they want: Not just what, but how the parts relate. Show the interaction between actors, causes, and consequences. Analysis goes deeper than explanation because it reveals the underlying structure of an issue.

"The stimulus reveals a structural tension: while the fund was created to assist vulnerable states, its voluntary nature means contributions depend on donor willingness, undermining the principle of climate justice it claims to uphold."

Climate Finance and the "Loss and Damage" Debate

At COP28 in Dubai (December 2023), 198 nations agreed to establish a Loss and Damage Fund to channel financial support to countries most vulnerable to climate change impacts. The fund, hosted by the World Bank on an interim basis, was designed to help developing nations recover from climate-related disasters such as floods, droughts, and rising sea levels that they lack the resources to address alone.

By late 2024, total pledges to the fund had reached approximately $700 million, far short of the estimated $400 billion per year needed according to research published in Nature Climate Change. The United States pledged $17.5 million, while the European Union collectively committed $275 million. China, now the world's largest emitter, classified itself as a developing nation and made no binding pledge, though it offered a voluntary $10 million contribution.

"The Loss and Damage Fund is a historic victory in principle, but a betrayal in practice. When the richest nations pledge pocket change while our islands disappear, we must ask: whose climate justice is this?" Tina Stege, Climate Envoy, Republic of the Marshall Islands, 2024

Critics argue that housing the fund at the World Bank, an institution where voting power is proportional to financial contribution, ensures that donor nations retain structural control over how funds are distributed. Small island developing states (SIDS) and least developed countries (LDCs), which bear the greatest climate burden, have the least influence over the fund's governance. At COP29 in Baku (November 2024), developing nations pushed for a new target of $1.3 trillion in annual climate finance by 2035, but the final agreement set a target of $300 billion, which many climate-vulnerable states rejected as inadequate.

Prompt A: Suggest

"Suggest three possible reasons why the Loss and Damage Fund has struggled to meet its targets."

2/3

Medium Response

2 out of 3 marks · Partially successful
📝 Student Response

This stimulus relates to the Environment GPC, specifically the issue of climate finance. One reason the Loss and Damage Fund has struggled is that major emitters like the United States have pledged very little money. The US pledged only $17.5 million, which is a very small amount for the world's richest country. ✓ Mark 1: valid reason with stimulus evidence

Another reason is that China has classified itself as a developing nation even though it is the world's largest emitter, so it did not have to make a binding pledge. This shows that the system allows major polluters to avoid responsibility. ✓ Mark 2: second valid reason with explanation

Finally, the fund has not received enough money because many countries do not want to pay for climate damage. ✗ Mark 3: too vague, no own knowledge, restates the problem

⚖️ Examiner Verdict: 2/3

What worked:

  • Points 1 and 2 are well-grounded in the stimulus with specific data ($17.5M, China's classification)
  • Point 2 goes beyond naming the issue and explains why the classification matters (avoidance of binding commitment)

Why it lost a mark:

  • Point 3 is a general restatement of the problem, not a distinct reason. "Countries don't want to pay" doesn't demonstrate any own knowledge from HL studies
  • The student could have used the stimulus to trigger thinking about concepts like structural power (World Bank governance giving donors control) or the democratic deficit in international institutions, showing understanding beyond the text
  • Even Points 1 and 2, while correct, mostly repeat stimulus content rather than adding analytical insight from the student's own HL knowledge

Elevation move: Use the stimulus as a springboard. Name the HL concept your point connects to (e.g., CBDR, structural power, sovereignty) and explain why it matters. This shows the examiner you understand the political dynamics, not just the facts in the text.

3/3

High Response

3 out of 3 marks · Full marks
📝 Student Response

This stimulus addresses the Environment GPC, focusing on the governance gap in climate finance. First, the fund has struggled because the principle of Common But Differentiated Responsibilities (CBDR), which underpins climate negotiations, creates no binding enforcement mechanism. The stimulus shows that the US pledged just $17.5 million against an estimated $400 billion annual need. CBDR recognizes that wealthy nations bear greater responsibility, but without a compliance mechanism, pledges remain voluntary, meaning the principle fails to translate moral obligation into financial action. ✓ Mark 1: names CBDR (own knowledge) and uses stimulus data to show why it fails in practice

Second, the UNFCCC's outdated binary classification system undermines the fund. China, the world's largest current emitter, classified itself as a developing nation and contributed only $10 million voluntarily. This demonstrates a flaw in the 1992 framework, which was designed when China's emissions were a fraction of current levels. The binary does not account for emerging economies that are both developing and major polluters. ✓ Mark 2: applies own knowledge of the 1992 UNFCCC framework to explain a structural loophole

Third, housing the fund at the World Bank creates what political scientists call a democratic deficit in global governance. Voting power is proportional to financial contribution, so donor nations retain structural control over disbursement. SIDS and LDCs, which bear the greatest climate burden, have the least voice. This mirrors broader critiques of Bretton Woods institutions as instruments of structural power rather than equitable multilateral cooperation. ✓ Mark 3: applies concepts of democratic deficit and structural power (own knowledge) to stimulus context

✅ Examiner Verdict: 3/3

Why this earns full marks:

  • Three genuinely distinct reasons, each addressing a different dimension (CBDR enforcement gap, classification framework, democratic deficit)
  • Clear own knowledge throughout. The student names specific HL concepts: CBDR, the 1992 UNFCCC framework, democratic deficit, structural power, and Bretton Woods critiques. These are not in the stimulus
  • Uses the stimulus as a springboard, not a script. Stimulus data ($17.5M, $10M, World Bank governance) triggers analytical points, but the student's own understanding drives the argument
  • Concise at approximately 180 words total, well within the 8-minute time budget

What makes this "high" rather than just "correct": The student names the political concepts behind the problems (CBDR, democratic deficit, structural power) instead of just describing what the stimulus says. This shows the examiner that the student understands the underlying political dynamics from their own HL studies, which is exactly what Q1 rewards.

Prompt B: Distinguish

"Distinguish between the perspectives of developed and developing nations on climate finance, as presented in the stimulus."

2/3

Medium Response

2 out of 3 marks · Partially successful
📝 Student Response

Within the Environment GPC, developed and developing nations hold fundamentally different views on climate finance. Developed nations like the United States view climate finance as a voluntary contribution. The US pledged only $17.5 million to the Loss and Damage Fund, suggesting that wealthy nations see this as discretionary rather than obligatory. ✓ Mark 1: valid distinction with stimulus evidence

In contrast, developing nations see climate finance as a matter of survival. Tina Stege, the Climate Envoy of the Marshall Islands, called the fund "a betrayal in practice" because the pledges are too small to address the real needs of vulnerable countries. ✓ Mark 2: clear contrast with quote evidence

Another difference is that developed countries have not committed enough money, whereas developing countries need much more funding. ✗ Mark 3: restates the same distinction from Point 1 without a new dimension

⚖️ Examiner Verdict: 2/3

What worked:

  • Points 1 and 2 present a clear developed vs. developing contrast, each supported by specific stimulus evidence ($17.5M pledge, Stege quote)
  • The student uses comparative language ("in contrast") which is appropriate for a "distinguish" question

Why it lost a mark:

  • Point 3 repeats the financial gap already covered in Point 1. "Not committed enough" vs. "need much more" is the same idea rephrased
  • The response also lacks own knowledge. Neither point names an HL concept (e.g., CBDR, structural power, democratic deficit). The student describes what the stimulus says but does not demonstrate understanding from their own study
  • A third dimension could be governance power (World Bank voting structure as a democratic deficit) or climate justice framing (developing nations viewing finance as reparations, not aid)

Elevation move: Replace Point 3 with a genuinely new dimension. Name the HL concept behind it (e.g., "This reflects the concept of structural power in global governance"). Show the examiner you understand the politics, not just the text.

3/3

High Response

3 out of 3 marks · Full marks
📝 Student Response

The stimulus sits within the Environment GPC and intersects with Equality and Poverty. First, developed and developing nations differ on the principle of Common But Differentiated Responsibilities (CBDR). Developed nations interpret CBDR as a basis for voluntary, discretionary contributions; the US pledged just $17.5 million. Developing nations see CBDR as establishing a legal and moral obligation, demanding $1.3 trillion annually at COP29 because they view climate finance as compensation owed by the historical polluters who caused the crisis. ✓ Mark 1: names CBDR (own knowledge) and shows how each side interprets it differently

Second, they differ on governance legitimacy. Developed nations accept the World Bank's donor-weighted voting structure, which reflects a liberal institutionalist view that financial contribution should determine influence. Developing nations, particularly SIDS and LDCs, see this as a democratic deficit where the most climate-vulnerable states have the least voice in how funds are distributed. ✓ Mark 2: applies democratic deficit concept (own knowledge) to distinguish governance perspectives

Third, the two groups frame the issue through different lenses. Developed nations treat climate finance as foreign aid, implying generosity. Developing nations frame it as climate justice, arguing that wealthy nations owe reparations for historical emissions. Tina Stege's description of the fund as "a betrayal in practice" reflects a postcolonial critique: the nations that caused the crisis are setting the terms of the response. ✓ Mark 3: applies climate justice and postcolonial critique frameworks (own knowledge)

✅ Examiner Verdict: 3/3

Why this earns full marks:

  • Three genuinely distinct dimensions of contrast: CBDR interpretation, governance legitimacy, and ideological framing (aid vs. justice)
  • Clear own knowledge throughout. The student names CBDR, democratic deficit, liberal institutionalism, climate justice, and postcolonial critique. None of these concepts are in the stimulus
  • Both sides represented in every point. Each distinction shows how the same issue (e.g., CBDR, World Bank governance) is interpreted differently by each group
  • Concise at approximately 180 words total, well within the 8-minute time budget

What makes this "high" rather than just "correct": The student names the political frameworks behind each perspective (CBDR, democratic deficit, climate justice, postcolonial critique) instead of just describing what each side wants. This shows the examiner that the student understands the theoretical underpinnings from their own HL studies, not just the surface-level content of the stimulus.

🔑

The Difference Between 2/3 and 3/3

The medium responses fall back on vague general claims that repeat the stimulus without adding insight. The high responses use the stimulus to trigger original thinking, then demonstrate understanding through their own knowledge of HL topics. Remember: the stimulus stimulates your thinking, but repeating its content will not earn high marks. Your own analytical understanding is what earns marks.

Q1-Specific TrapsCommon Pitfalls

✅ Do This

  • Read the question before the stimulus so you know what you're looking for
  • Match your response structure to the command term (e.g., "distinguish" = comparison, "suggest" = separate points)
  • Use the stimulus to trigger your thinking, then apply your own HL knowledge
  • Reference specific elements from the stimulus as they relate to your HL topics
  • Keep it under 150 words total to save time for Q2 and Q3
  • Number your points if asked for multiple (makes it easy for the examiner to award marks)

❌ Don't Do This

  • Write a full essay. Q1 rewards efficiency, not length
  • Simply repeat or paraphrase the stimulus. This is not a source interpretation task and repeating content will not earn high marks
  • Give overlapping points that are really the same idea twice
  • Use the wrong command term approach. "Suggesting" when asked to "distinguish" will lose marks even if your content is correct
  • Leave a vague third point and hope the examiner is generous
  • Spend more than 8 minutes because every extra minute comes from your Q3 time
🚨

The #1 Q1 Mistake

"Repackaging the same point in different words." If the question asks for three reasons and you give one reason three different ways, you get 1 mark, not 3. Each point must address a genuinely different dimension of the issue. Before writing your final point, ask yourself: "Is this actually new, or am I just rewording Point 1?"
