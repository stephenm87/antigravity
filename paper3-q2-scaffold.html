1
Q2a: Case Study AnalysisWhat the Examiner Wants

Q2a is worth 4 marks and uses band descriptors (not point-marking). It tests your demonstration of knowledge, understanding, and analysis of an identified political issue referred to in the stimulus. You demonstrate this by using your researched case studies to explain the issue. You might:

  • Use a case study to explain tensions or gaps between national decision-making and global solutions
  • Explain a term used in the stimulus through your case study
  • Explain a claim expressed by the stimulus using your own research
  • Consider the role of stakeholders or actors relevant to the political issue

Band 1–2 · 1–2 marks

Demands partially addressed. Response is mostly descriptive. Some knowledge of the political issue is demonstrated, but it is not all relevant or accurate.

Band 3–4 · 3–4 marks

Demands of the question are addressed. Response provides a clear analysis of a political issue. Relevant and accurate knowledge of the context is demonstrated.

📋

Key Exam Structure: The Framing Statement

Before Q2a and Q2b are posed, there will be a statement about the stimulus material that highlights a specific idea. Read this carefully because it tells you what lens to apply to your case study. Both parts (a) and (b) require you to refer to a relevant case study. Choose one that enables you to respond to both parts using convincing detail.

⚠️

Do Not Assume the Examiner Knows Your Case Study

Make sure you give clear factual details in your response. The question specifies a certain number of links that need to be explained. Name your case study precisely, provide dates, actors, and outcomes. The examiner knows nothing about your specific research.

2
Q2b: Policy RecommendationWhat the Examiner Wants

Q2b is worth 6 marks and is band-marked. It tests your ability to develop a coherent and relevant policy, course of action, or solution to the identified political issue. Your response must build on your Q2a response. You can do this by:

  • Recommending how a government could work with other stakeholders to address the challenge
  • Recommending a course of action that would increase the influence of a specific state or non-state actor
  • Making a recommendation for how global governance could be improved to address political challenges more effectively

Band 1–2 · 1–2 marks

A recommendation is presented, but it is vague or unclear. The recommendation does not clearly address the identified political issue.

Band 3–4 · 3–4 marks

An adequate recommendation is presented. The recommendation addresses the identified political issue. Possible challenges or implications are not considered.

Band 5–6 · 5–6 marks

A clear and well-supported recommendation is presented. The recommendation addresses the identified political issue effectively. Possible challenges, implications, or unintended consequences are considered.

🏛

Evidence Over Hypothetical Thinking

Your policy recommendation will be considered well-supported if you provide specific evidence of similar policies from your case studies. The textbook is explicit: reasoning must be based on evidence, rather than hypothetical thinking. Reference:

  • Previous actions taken in your chosen case or other relevant cases you have studied
  • Theoretical approaches or models that favour certain approaches for certain issues
  • Frameworks, mechanisms, or agreements already in place that should facilitate implementation
🔗

Q2b Builds on Q2a

Your Q2b policy must directly address the challenge you identified in Q2a. This is why choosing one case study that works for both parts is critical. Your Q2b reasoning should cite evidence from your Q2a case study (and other cases you have studied) rather than introducing a completely new context.

3
Time ManagementStrategy for Q2a + Q2b

The textbook recommends 12 minutes for Q2a and 20 minutes for Q2b (32 minutes combined). Q2a is shorter and more factual. Q2b demands more development because it requires a well-supported policy with evidence and consideration of challenges. Do not spend more than 15 minutes on Q2a or you will run out of time for Q2b and Q3.

📏

Target Word Counts

Q2a should be approximately 150–200 words — enough for context, one piece of specific evidence, and a bridge sentence. Q2b should be approximately 250–350 words — enough for a named actor, detailed mechanism, evidence-backed rationale, and risk/mitigation. Going significantly over these targets usually means you're spending too long and will run out of time for Q3.

The Loss and Damage Fund: Climate Finance at a Crossroads

At COP28 in December 2023, 198 nations formally launched the Loss and Damage Fund to compensate developing nations for climate-related destruction. Within hours, pledges totaled approximately $700 million. Analysts noted that this figure represents less than 0.2% of the estimated $400 billion in annual climate-related damages suffered by developing nations.

The United States, the world's largest historical emitter, pledged $17.5 million. China, currently the world's largest annual emitter, classified itself as a developing nation under the UNFCCC framework and contributed a voluntary $10 million. The fund was placed under the interim trusteeship of the World Bank, where voting power is weighted by financial contribution. Small Island Developing States (SIDS) and Least Developed Countries (LDCs), which bear the greatest climate impacts, hold minimal governance influence.

"This fund was supposed to be a lifeline. Instead, it is a betrayal in practice. The nations drowning are being asked to be grateful for pennies from the nations that caused the flood." Tina Stege, Climate Envoy, Republic of the Marshall Islands (2024)

At COP29 in November 2024, developing nations demanded a new collective target of $1.3 trillion per year by 2035 to address loss and damage, adaptation, and mitigation. Developed nations offered $300 billion. The compromise collapsed when the African Group walked out, calling the offer "an insult to our continent." The final agreement set a $300 billion annual target, which many observers described as a diplomatic failure for climate justice.

Q2a: Case Study Bridge

"Describe a political issue from a case study you have researched that illustrates similar tensions."

2/4

Medium Response

2 out of 4 marks · Band 1–2: mostly descriptive
📝 Student Response

Drawing from the Environment GPC, with connections to Poverty, a similar issue can be seen in the Amazon rainforest, where deforestation is a major political challenge. Many countries in South America are cutting down trees for farming and industry. ✓ Mark 1: identifies a political issue (deforestation)

The Brazilian government under Bolsonaro reduced environmental protections, allowing ranchers and mining companies to clear large areas of the Amazon. This caused international criticism from NGOs like Greenpeace and from European leaders. ✓ Mark 2: provides some context with actors

Deforestation is bad for the environment because the Amazon produces a lot of the world's oxygen and stores carbon. This means it affects everyone, not just Brazil. ✗ Mark 3: no specific evidence (statistic, date, or outcome) ✗ Mark 4: no bridge back to the stimulus

⚖️ Examiner Verdict: 2/4 (Band 1–2)

What worked:

  • The case study is relevant and the student names specific actors (Bolsonaro, ranchers, Greenpeace)
  • There is some descriptive context about what happened

Why it scored Band 1–2:

  • Mostly descriptive. The response describes what happened but does not provide clear analysis of the political issue. It lists facts without explaining the underlying tension
  • Knowledge is not all relevant or accurate. "Large areas" and "a lot of oxygen" are vague. Specific, accurate evidence (e.g., 13,235 km² deforested in 2021) would demonstrate stronger knowledge
  • No link back to the stimulus. The framing statement before Q2 highlights a specific idea from the stimulus. This response never connects the case study to that theme

Elevation move: Move beyond description. Analyze why the issue matters politically (tension between sovereignty and global environmental governance). Add specific, accurate evidence. Connect back to the stimulus theme.

4/4

High Response

4 out of 4 marks · Band 3–4: clear analysis
📝 Student Response

Drawing from the Environment GPC, with clear intersections to Poverty and Equality, the tensions in the stimulus mirror those seen in the Cochabamba Water War in Bolivia (1999–2000), where the privatization of Cochabamba's municipal water system created a direct conflict between economic development and community consent. ✓ Mark 1: specific political issue identified with date and location

Under pressure from the World Bank, the Bolivian government granted a 40-year concession to Aguas del Tunari, a subsidiary of the US-based multinational Bechtel Corporation. Water prices rose by up to 200% within weeks, making basic water access unaffordable for many of the city's poorest residents. Protests led by the Coordinadora de Defensa del Agua brought tens of thousands to the streets, resulting in a state of emergency, several deaths, and the eventual cancellation of the contract in April 2000. ✓ Mark 2: detailed context with actors (World Bank, Bechtel, Coordinadora) ✓ Mark 3: specific evidence (200% price increase, 40-year concession, April 2000 cancellation)

Like the climate finance dispute in the stimulus, the Cochabamba case illustrates how decisions made by powerful external actors (in this case the World Bank and Bechtel, in the stimulus case the UNFCCC and donor states) can exclude the communities most affected from meaningful participation in governance. In both cases, the fundamental tension is between economic or institutional efficiency and the right of affected populations to shape the policies that determine their survival. ✓ Mark 4: explicit bridge linking case study to stimulus theme

✅ Examiner Verdict: 4/4 (Band 3–4)

Why this earns full marks:

  • Clear analysis, not just description. The response doesn't just say what happened; it explains why it matters (exclusion from governance, efficiency vs. participation rights)
  • Relevant and accurate knowledge: specific actors (World Bank, Bechtel, Coordinadora), precise evidence (200% price increase, 40-year concession, April 2000 cancellation)
  • Does not assume examiner knowledge. The case study is fully explained: who, what, where, when, and why, with clear factual details
  • Links back to stimulus theme: the final paragraph draws a direct, analytical parallel to the stimulus, naming equivalent actors and the shared structural tension

What makes this Band 3–4 rather than Band 1–2: The student goes beyond description to provide clear analysis of the political issue. They identify the structural parallel (external actors excluding affected communities from governance) and explain why this case study is relevant to the stimulus theme, demonstrating genuine understanding rather than surface-level recall.

High Response — Alt. Case Study

4 out of 4 marks · Band 3–4 · Different GPC
📝 Student Response

Drawing from the Poverty GPC, with intersections to Health and Environment, the tensions in the stimulus are mirrored by the cobalt mining crisis in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), where the extraction of a critical resource for the global green energy transition is built on conditions that contradict the very sustainability goals it claims to serve. ✓ Mark 1: specific political issue with analytical framing

The DRC holds approximately 70% of global cobalt reserves, essential for lithium-ion batteries in electric vehicles and smartphones. Multinational corporations such as Glencore and China Molybdenum operate large-scale industrial mines, while an estimated 40,000 children work in artisanal cobalt mines according to a 2019 Amnesty International report. Despite cobalt exports worth over $24 billion, the DRC's HDI remains 0.479 (2023), ranking 179th out of 191 countries. ✓ Mark 2: detailed context with specific actors ✓ Mark 3: precise evidence (70%, 40,000 children, $24B, HDI 0.479)

Like the climate finance dispute in the stimulus, where SIDS and LDCs bear the greatest climate impacts but hold minimal governance influence over the fund, the DRC bears the greatest human and environmental costs of cobalt extraction while receiving a fraction of the value chain's profits. In both cases, the structural inequality is identical: those who suffer most have the least power to shape the rules of engagement. ✓ Mark 4: explicit bridge linking case study to stimulus theme

✅ Examiner Verdict: 4/4 (Band 3–4)

Why this works with a different GPC:

  • Uses the Poverty GPC rather than Environment, showing students that multiple case studies can map onto the same stimulus
  • Provides even more quantitative evidence than the Cochabamba exemplar — four distinct data points in one paragraph
  • The bridge sentence mirrors the stimulus structure precisely (those who suffer most → least governance power)
  • Demonstrates that a development/sustainability case study can connect to a climate finance stimulus through the shared theme of structural inequality

Q2b: Policy Recommendation

"Propose one detailed policy recommendation to address the challenge identified in Q2a."

3/6

Medium Response

3 out of 6 marks · Band 3–4
📝 Student Response

ACTOR Building on my Q2a analysis of the Cochabamba Water War (Environment / Poverty GPCs), the United Nations should take action to prevent future cases like this. ✓ Actor identified, but vague

MECHANISM Specifically, the UN should create rules that require governments to consult with local communities before privatizing public services. This would make sure people have a voice in decisions that affect them. ✓ Mechanism present but generic

RATIONALE This policy would work because the Cochabamba protests showed that people will resist privatization when they are not consulted. If governments are required to consult, they are more likely to create fair policies. ✓ Basic rationale with case study reference

Overall, the UN needs to protect the rights of communities in developing countries from exploitation by multinational corporations. ✗ No risk analysis or mitigation

⚖️ Examiner Verdict: 3/6 (Band 3–4)

What worked:

  • An adequate recommendation is presented that addresses the identified political issue
  • There is an attempt at justification using case study evidence ("protests showed that people will resist")

Why it scored Band 3–4:

  • Possible challenges or implications are not considered. This is the key Band 3–4 marker from the rubric. The student never addresses what could go wrong
  • The recommendation is adequate but not well-supported. "The UN should create rules" is vague. A well-supported recommendation would name the specific body, instrument, and cite evidence of similar policies
  • Reasoning is partially hypothetical. "If governments are required to consult, they are more likely to create fair policies" is speculative. The textbook requires reasoning based on evidence, not hypothetical thinking

Elevation move: To reach Band 5–6, add consideration of challenges and implications. Support your rationale with evidence from your case study or other cases (not hypothetical reasoning). Name the specific body and instrument.

6/6

High Response

6 out of 6 marks · Band 5–6
📝 Student Response

ACTOR Building on my Q2a case study within the Environment / Poverty / Equality GPCs, the UN Human Rights Council (UNHRC), acting through its Special Rapporteur on the Human Right to Safe Drinking Water and Sanitation, should develop a Binding Protocol on Prior Community Consent for Essential Service Privatization. ✓ Specific actor with institutional detail

MECHANISM The protocol would require any state seeking World Bank or IMF structural adjustment lending to first conduct an independent, UNHRC-verified community impact assessment for any proposed privatization of water, energy, or healthcare services. States that fail to demonstrate meaningful community consent, defined as informed consultation with affected populations and a formal public comment period of at least 90 days, would be ineligible for concessional lending terms. The protocol would build on the existing framework of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) established in the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples. ✓ Detailed, actionable mechanism with legal precedent

RATIONALE This policy is feasible because it uses an existing enforcement lever: conditionality on lending. The World Bank already attaches governance conditions to its loans. In Cochabamba, the privatization was itself a World Bank condition, meaning the institution has the infrastructure to reverse this dynamic. The FPIC framework provides legal precedent, and the UNHRC Special Rapporteur has already issued reports (2010, 2014) identifying water privatization without consent as a human rights violation. ✓ Evidence-backed rationale with precedent

RISK However, this policy risks resistance from states that view lending conditionality as an infringement on sovereignty, particularly in the Global South where structural adjustment has a colonial legacy. Developing nations may see UNHRC-mandated assessments as another layer of Western institutional control over their domestic policy decisions. ✓ Identifies a specific, credible risk

MITIGATION To mitigate this, the protocol should be co-designed with the G77 bloc and regional development banks (AfDB, ADB) rather than imposed top-down. Assessment teams should be drawn from regional experts, not Geneva-based staff. This localizes the oversight mechanism and reduces the perception of neocolonial conditionality, making compliance politically viable for borrowing states. ✓ Concrete mitigation addressing the specific risk

✅ Examiner Verdict: 6/6 (Band 5–6)

Why this earns top band:

  • Clear and well-supported recommendation that addresses the identified political issue effectively
  • Evidence-based reasoning throughout: cites World Bank conditionality infrastructure, FPIC legal precedent, existing Special Rapporteur reports (2010, 2014). No hypothetical thinking
  • Builds directly on Q2a: the policy addresses the exact challenge (water privatization without consent) identified in the case study
  • References frameworks already in place: FPIC, World Bank lending conditions, UNHRC Special Rapporteur mandate
  • Possible challenges, implications, and unintended consequences ARE considered: sovereignty concerns, neocolonial perception, and a concrete mitigation (G77 co-design, regional assessment teams)

What makes this Band 5–6: The consideration of challenges and unintended consequences is what separates Band 5–6 from Band 3–4. This student doesn't just propose a policy; they think about why it could fail (sovereignty objections, neocolonial perception) and how to address that (co-design with G77, localized oversight). All reasoning is based on evidence from the case study and existing frameworks, not hypothetical thinking.

🔑

The Difference Between Band 3–4 and Band 5–6

Both students propose a UN-led policy to prevent exploitative privatization. The textbook rubric makes the distinction clear: Band 3–4 does not consider possible challenges or implications. Band 5–6 does. Additionally, the high response uses evidence-based reasoning throughout (citing existing frameworks, precedent, and case study evidence) rather than hypothetical thinking. Band 5–6 students think about why their policy could fail, not just why it should succeed.

Common TrapsPitfalls for Q2a + Q2b

Q2a: Case Study

✅ Do This

  • Name your case study in the first sentence with location and date
  • Include at least one specific statistic, date, or outcome
  • End with a bridge sentence connecting your case study to the stimulus theme
  • Name specific actors (state, IGO, NGO, MNC) and their roles
  • Choose the case study that best fits the stimulus, not your favorite one

❌ Don't Do This

  • Write about a topic so broad it could apply to anything ("inequality in Africa")
  • Describe the case study at length without ever linking it to the stimulus
  • Rely on general knowledge instead of specific evidence from your research
  • Spend 20+ minutes on Q2a, leaving too little time for Q2b and Q3
  • Choose a case study from a GPC that has no connection to the stimulus theme

Q2b: Policy Recommendation

✅ Do This

  • Name a specific actor: a named institution, body, or mechanism (not "the international community")
  • Describe a concrete action: treaty clause, fund, regulatory amendment, or deployment
  • Justify using evidence, not hypothetical thinking: cite similar policies from your case studies, existing frameworks, or theoretical models
  • Consider challenges, implications, or unintended consequences to reach Band 5–6
  • Connect your policy back to the issue identified in Q2a

❌ Don't Do This

  • Propose a policy so vague it could apply to any issue ("countries should cooperate more")
  • Forget to name who implements the policy (Band 1–2 territory)
  • Write three separate mini-policies instead of one detailed recommendation
  • Treat challenges/implications as optional (considering them is what separates Band 3–4 from Band 5–6)
  • Use hypothetical reasoning ("if they did X, then Y would happen") instead of evidence-based reasoning from your case studies
🚨

The #1 Q2b Mistake

"The UN should do more." This is the most common Band 1–2 response. The examiner cannot award marks for a vague wish. Replace "the UN" with a specific body, and replace "do more" with a specific mechanism. One concrete policy proposal will always outscore three vague suggestions.

📚

Theory Toolbox for Q2b Rationale

The rubric rewards reasoning based on theoretical approaches or models. When writing your Q2b rationale, consider grounding your policy in one of the core development theories: Modernization Theory (free markets, trade integration), Dependency Theory (structural disadvantage, unequal exchange), Core-Periphery Model (systemic inequality), or Sustainable Development (balancing economic, social, and environmental goals).
