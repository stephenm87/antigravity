Service Learning Project

SLP Expectations Guide

Getting the details right. Your project is only as strong as the clarity, honesty, and precision of the work behind it.

1
Know Your Own Project The 5W + 1H Framework

Before you create your infographic, you should be able to answer each of these clearly and specifically. If your answer sounds vague ("we helped some people"), you haven't defined it well enough.

Who
Who is involved and who benefits?
Name your team members, your community partner (if any), and the specific group you're serving. "The community" is too vague — say who exactly.
What
What exactly are you doing?
Describe the specific action. Not "raising awareness" — say what form it takes. A workshop? A survey? A donation drive? Name the deliverable.
Where
Where does this take place?
The school? A local organization? Online? Be specific — if it's a community center, name it. If it's a school event, say which one.
When
What is the timeline?
When did you start planning? When did you execute? When did you collect data? Include actual dates — "this semester" is not a timeline.
Why
Why does this matter?
What is the need or problem you're addressing? Don't just say it's important — explain why this specific community or group needs this specific action right now.
How
How did you carry it out?
Walk through the process step by step. What tools, strategies, or methods did you use? What decisions did you make and why? This feeds directly into your Infographic 2 (My Process).

Too Vague

"We helped people at our school learn about recycling by doing a project."

Specific and Clear

"Our team of 3 organized two lunch-break recycling workshops for Grade 9 students in the school cafeteria on Feb 12 and 19, with a pre- and post-survey to measure awareness changes."

2
Measuring Your Impact Data Collection and Methodology

Your Infographic 1 (My Impact) requires data. But "data" doesn't mean you need hundreds of responses — it means you need a clear method, honest numbers, and accurate reporting.

What counts as data?

  • Survey responses (Google Forms, paper surveys)
  • Before/after measurements (awareness scores, skill tests, behavior counts)
  • Attendance records (who showed up, how many participated)
  • Observation logs (what you noticed changing over time)
  • Interviews or quotes from participants
  • Physical measurements (kilograms collected, hours volunteered, items donated)

Defining your methodology

You need to be able to answer these 4 questions clearly. If you can't, your data isn't reliable enough to present.

Question What you need to state Example
What did you measure? The specific variable or outcome "Student awareness of recycling categories"
How did you measure it? The tool or instrument "A 5-question Google Form survey"
When did you measure it? Timing (before, during, after) "Pre-workshop on Feb 10 and post-workshop on Feb 19"
Who did you measure? Sample size and group "14 Grade 9 students who attended both sessions"

Small groups are fine. If only 8 people participated, report 8. Don't say "we surveyed the school" if you surveyed 8 people. Small data is valid when reported honestly — faking scale is not.

3
If Using a Google Form Writing Good Survey Questions

Most SLP surveys are too vague to produce usable data. Good questions are specific, answer-able, and tied to what you're actually trying to measure. Here are examples you can adapt:

Pre/Post Awareness Questions

1
Before this workshop, how confident were you sorting recyclable vs. non-recyclable items?
Scale: 1 (Not at all confident) to 5 (Very confident)
2
Which of the following items can go in the recycling bin at our school? (Select all that apply)
Multiple choice — tests actual knowledge, not just feelings
  • Plastic water bottles
  • Food-stained pizza boxes
  • Aluminum cans
  • Tissues and napkins

Behavior Change Questions

3
In the past week, how many times did you check whether an item was recyclable before throwing it away?
Multiple choice
  • Never
  • 1–2 times
  • 3–5 times
  • Every time
4
After attending this session, will you do anything differently? If yes, what?
Short answer — captures qualitative data and quotes for your infographic

Feedback / Reflection Questions

5
What was the most useful thing you learned today?
Short answer — great for pulling direct quotes
6
How would you rate this session overall?
Scale: 1 (Not helpful) to 5 (Very helpful)

Weak Survey Question

"Did you like our project?"

"Do you think recycling is important?"

(Leading, vague, everyone says yes — tells you nothing)

Strong Survey Question

"After this workshop, can you name 3 items that cannot be recycled at our school?"

"How many times this week did you separate waste before disposal?"

(Specific, measurable, tests real knowledge or behavior)

Pro tip: Use the same questions for your pre-survey and post-survey. That's how you show change. If the questions are different, you can't compare results.

4
Non-Negotiable Accuracy and Honesty in Your Data

This is where many SLP projects lose credibility. Whether your audience is 5 people or 500, honesty in reporting is not optional. Here are the expectations:

1

Report your actual numbers

If 6 people participated, say 6. Do not round up, exaggerate, or use vague language like "many" or "the community." Small numbers are completely acceptable — inflated numbers are not.

2

Small groups are real data

A well-designed survey of 8 people is more valuable than a sloppy survey of 80. Your infographic should show that you were thoughtful in how you collected and interpreted responses — not that you reached a huge number.

3

Show your work, not just results

Don't just say "75% of people agreed." Say: "6 out of 8 respondents selected 'Agree' or 'Strongly Agree' on Question 3." Include how many people you surveyed, when, and how.

4

Mixed or negative results are fine

If your workshop didn't change anyone's mind, say so — and reflect on why. That's more impressive than fabricating a success story. Real learning comes from honest analysis.

5

Don't present opinions as data

"I think people learned a lot" is a reflection. "5 out of 7 participants scored higher on the post-quiz than the pre-quiz" is data. Your infographic needs the second type.

Academic integrity applies here. Fabricating survey results, inflating participation numbers, or presenting work you didn't actually do is a violation — even in a service project. Treat this like any other assessment.

5
Quick Reference Self-Check Before You Submit

Can you pass the Detail Test?

Read each statement below. If you can't honestly answer yes, you need to go back and fix that part of your project.

Expectation Ask Yourself
Project clarity Could a stranger read my infographic and understand exactly what I did, for whom, where, and when?
Methodology Can I explain how I collected my data in 2 sentences or less?
Data accuracy Are all my numbers real? Do I report my actual sample size?
Survey quality Do my survey questions measure something specific — not just "did you enjoy this?"
Evidence over opinion Am I presenting what the data shows, not what I wish it showed?
Honest reflection If something didn't work, did I say so and explain what I learned?

The Bottom Line

Details matter. A strong SLP project isn't one that sounds impressive — it's one where every claim is backed by something real. Clear descriptions, honest numbers, thoughtful surveys, and accurate reporting are what separate good work from surface-level work.

You don't need a huge sample or a perfect outcome. You need to show that you were intentional, methodical, and truthful about what you did and what happened.
