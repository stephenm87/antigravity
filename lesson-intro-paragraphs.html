Paper 1 — Source Analysis Introduction

1h 15m · 25 Marks · Focus: 10-mark extended question

Step 1 — Learn the Formula

Paper 1 gives you stimulus sources and asks you to analyse them. Your introduction for the 10-mark question must orient the examiner to the source material and your analytical direction.

Slot 1Context Anchor
+
Slot 2Source Position
+
Slot 3Analytical Roadmap
1

Context Anchor

In 1–2 sentences, establish the broad political issue the sources address. Use an IB core concept to ground the reader.

2

Source Position

Identify the key tension or disagreement between the sources. Show how they contrast — don't summarise each one individually.

3

Analytical Roadmap

Signal your evaluative direction: what will you explore, and what is your preliminary conclusion?

Step 2 — Read the Stimulus Sources

The following sources relate to the question of sovereignty and international intervention.

Source A — State Sovereignty as a Shield

"The principle of non-intervention remains the cornerstone of the international order. States that respect each other's territorial integrity create a stable system; those that intervene — however noble the stated objective — undermine the very structure that prevents global chaos. The Iraq War of 2003 demonstrated that intervention without Security Council authorisation sets a dangerous precedent, enabling powerful states to impose regime change under the guise of humanitarianism.

Furthermore, the aftermath of the NATO-led intervention in Libya in 2011 illustrates the long-term destabilisation that regime change can produce. While the intervention was justified under R2P principles and authorised by UN Security Council Resolution 1973, the resulting power vacuum led to a decade of civil war, the proliferation of weapons across the Sahel, and the emergence of rival governments. Critics argue that intervention rarely achieves its stated humanitarian objectives and instead creates new security threats, reinforcing the realist position that sovereignty — however imperfect — provides a more stable foundation for international order than selective, power-driven interference."

— Adapted from a realist policy analysis, International Security Review, 2024

Source B — Sovereignty as Responsibility

"When a state systematically massacres its own people, the international community cannot hide behind sovereignty. The Responsibility to Protect (R2P) doctrine, endorsed by the UN General Assembly in 2005, recognises that sovereignty is not a right but a responsibility. Rwanda's genocide in 1994, where 800,000 people were killed in 100 days while the world watched, proved the catastrophic cost of inaction. Sovereignty must be conditional.

The case of Kosovo in 1999 further demonstrates that intervention can succeed where the political will exists. NATO's air campaign, though conducted without Security Council authorisation due to the threat of a Russian veto, is widely credited with halting ethnic cleansing by Serbian forces. The subsequent establishment of an independent Kosovo, recognised by over 100 states, suggests that the international community can act as a legitimate guarantor of human rights when a state weaponises its sovereignty against its own population. The moral imperative, cosmopolitan scholars argue, must take precedence over procedural adherence to a sovereignty regime that was designed to protect states — not to shield perpetrators of mass atrocity."

— Adapted from a cosmopolitan commentary, Human Rights Quarterly, 2023

Source C — Data: UN Security Council Vetoes on Intervention (2011–2024)

Between 2011 and 2024, Russia and China used their veto power 14 times to block resolutions authorising humanitarian intervention or sanctions — including 6 vetoes on Syria alone. In the same period, only 3 intervention resolutions were successfully passed (Libya 2011, Central African Republic 2013, Mali 2013). The data suggests that the Security Council's structural design makes consistent, timely intervention nearly impossible.

A closer examination of the voting patterns reveals a deepening geopolitical divide. Russia's vetoes on Syria were explicitly framed as defending the sovereignty of the Assad government against Western-led regime change, while the United States and its allies characterised them as enabling mass atrocities. The result has been a paralysis that has eroded confidence in multilateral institutions: a 2023 International Crisis Group survey found that 67% of humanitarian organisations rated the Security Council as "ineffective or counterproductive" in responding to protection crises. Some analysts have called for reform — including veto restraint initiatives and the empowerment of the General Assembly through the "Uniting for Peace" mechanism — but structural change remains blocked by the very powers who benefit from the status quo.

— Compiled from UN Security Council records and International Crisis Group, 2023

Source D — Voices from Below: Civil Society and the Limits of State-Centric Frameworks

"The sovereignty debate, as framed by both its defenders and its critics, overwhelmingly centres on the actions and interests of states and intergovernmental organisations. What is consistently absent is the voice of affected populations themselves. In South Sudan, for example, the deployment of the UN Mission (UNMISS) in 2011 was framed as a protective intervention, yet years of peacekeeping have failed to prevent ongoing ethnic violence, sexual violence as a weapon of war, and the displacement of over 4 million people. The communities most affected by conflict rarely have meaningful input into decisions about intervention, ceasefire terms, or post-conflict governance.

Feminist and postcolonial scholars argue that this omission is not accidental but structural: the international order privileges the security of states over the security of individuals, and particularly over the security of women, children, and marginalised ethnic groups who bear the greatest burden of both conflict and intervention. A truly human-centred approach to sovereignty would require not just the right to protect, but the right to be heard — embedding local civil society, women's organisations, and indigenous communities into decision-making processes rather than treating them as passive beneficiaries of top-down intervention."

— Adapted from a feminist IR analysis, Gender & Global Politics, 2024

Prompt: "Using the sources, evaluate the extent to which state sovereignty is eroded by international intervention." [10 marks]

Step 3 — Study the High-Scoring Example

High-Scoring Paper 1 Intro

The question of whether sovereignty remains absolute or is conditional upon a state's protection of its citizens has become one of the defining tensions of 21st-century international relations, particularly as the Responsibility to Protect (R2P) doctrine has been both invoked and contested across cases from Libya to South Sudan. The sources reveal a multi-dimensional debate: Sources A and B present the core divide between statist defenders of non-interference and cosmopolitan advocates for conditional sovereignty, while Source C demonstrates that the Security Council's veto structure makes consistent intervention structurally improbable — and Source D challenges the entire framework by arguing that the state-centric debate systematically excludes the voices of affected populations. This response will evaluate these positions by analysing the tension between sovereignty as a stabilising principle and sovereignty as a shield for impunity, ultimately arguing that while sovereignty is not eroded in legal principle, its practical legitimacy is increasingly contested through institutional mechanisms, grassroots resistance, and the structural power politics that constrain multilateral action.

Step 4 — Diagnose Weak Introductions

Below are three poorly written introductions for the same prompt and sources. For each one, identify the missing formula slots and write what you would add to fix it.

"Source A talks about how sovereignty is important and intervention is bad. Source B talks about R2P and says sovereignty should be conditional. Source C shows some veto data. Source D says civil society voices are missing. These sources have different opinions about sovereignty and intervention."
Diagnosis: This introduction summarises each source individually but never identifies the tension between them (missing Slot 2: Source Position). It also lacks a Context Anchor — there is no broader political framing or IB concept. Finally, there is no Analytical Roadmap telling the examiner what direction the essay will take.
Your fix — What would you add or change? _____________________
"Sovereignty has been debated by many scholars for centuries. Some believe in absolute sovereignty while others think it should be limited. This is a complex topic with many perspectives. In this essay I will discuss whether sovereignty is eroded by intervention."
Diagnosis: This introduction is entirely generic — it could apply to any sovereignty question on any exam. It has no Context Anchor linked to the specific stimulus material, no reference to the sources at all (missing Source Position), and the "roadmap" is too vague to be useful. The phrase "I will discuss" signals description, not evaluation.
Your fix — What would you add or change? _____________________
"The Responsibility to Protect (R2P) shows that sovereignty must be conditional. As Source B argues, Rwanda proved that inaction is worse than intervention. The international community has a moral duty to act when states commit atrocities. This essay will show that sovereignty is completely eroded by intervention."
Diagnosis: This introduction only engages with one source (Source B) and one perspective. It completely ignores Source A's realist counter-argument, Source C's structural data on veto paralysis, and Source D's challenge to the state-centric framing — meaning the Source Position slot is one-sided. The thesis ("completely eroded") is absolutist, showing no evaluative nuance. A strong Paper 1 intro acknowledges the full debate across all sources, not just one side.

Paper 2 — Extended Response Introduction

1h 45m · 30 Marks · Focus: 15-mark essay (choose 1 of 2)

Step 1 — Learn the Formula

Paper 2 is a free-standing essay — no sources provided. You must bring your own knowledge, case studies, and theoretical frameworks. This is where the "Golden Thread" begins: your thesis runs through every paragraph.

Slot 1Concept Definition
+
Slot 2Tension Framing
+
Slot 3Case Preview
+
Slot 4Thesis Statement
1

Concept Definition

Define the central IB concept (legitimacy, sovereignty, power, etc.) in 1–2 sentences using IB-specific language, not a dictionary definition.

2

Tension Framing

Frame the core debate as a clash between two perspectives (e.g., realist vs. liberal, statist vs. cosmopolitan). This signals evaluation.

3

Case Preview

Name your two key case studies that will anchor your argument. Depth over breadth — the examiner wants sustained analysis.

4

Thesis Statement

State your main claim — the "Golden Thread." Use evaluative language: "This essay will argue…", "To a significant extent…"

Step 2 — Read the Prompt

Prompt: "Evaluate the claim that legitimacy is the most important factor in maintaining political order." [15 marks]

Step 3 — Study the High-Scoring Example

High-Scoring Paper 2 Intro

Legitimacy, fundamentally defined as the recognised right to rule and the acceptance of authority by those governed, is the invisible architecture upon which all political order is built. While realist scholars contend that it is raw power — particularly military and economic coercion — that truly sustains political order, constructivist and liberal perspectives argue that without the consent of the governed, even the most powerful regime is built on sand. This essay will examine this tension by synthesising the contrasting cases of the European Union's evolving democratic legitimacy crisis and the collapse of the Assad regime in Syria, where a prolonged legitimacy vacuum fuelled a devastating civil war. Ultimately, this essay will argue that while power can enforce short-term compliance, legitimacy is the essential foundation for durable political order, as its absence inevitably triggers resistance, fragmentation, and institutional decay.

Step 4 — Diagnose Weak Introductions

"Legitimacy means the right to govern. It is important for maintaining political order. Many countries have struggled with legitimacy throughout history. This essay will look at whether legitimacy is the most important factor."
Diagnosis: The Concept Definition is shallow — "the right to govern" lacks IB depth (no mention of consent, recognition, or competing sources of legitimacy). There is no Tension Framing (no competing perspectives). No Case Preview (no named cases). The thesis "will look at whether" is descriptive, not evaluative — it doesn't take a position.
Your fix — Rewrite this with all four slots: ___________________
"The EU has a democratic deficit because citizens feel disconnected from Brussels. In Syria, Assad lost legitimacy and there was a civil war. In Venezuela, Maduro's legitimacy has been questioned. In North Korea, the regime uses propaganda. These examples show legitimacy matters."
Diagnosis: This introduction lists four cases but analyses none — it's a Case Preview without the other three slots. There is no Concept Definition, no Tension Framing between perspectives, and the final sentence ("legitimacy matters") is not a genuine Thesis. Four cases is also too many — the examiner wants depth, not breadth.
Your fix — Pick 2 cases and add the missing slots: ____________
"There are many factors that contribute to political order, such as legitimacy, power, institutions, and ideology. All of these are important in different ways. Some scholars think legitimacy is the most important; others disagree. This essay will examine both sides of the debate."
Diagnosis: This introduction refuses to take a position — "both sides" and "all are important" signals a lack of evaluative thinking. While there is a vague Tension Framing, it names no specific theories or scholars. There is no Case Preview and no Thesis Statement. The examiner cannot tell what argument the essay will make.
Your fix — Take a clear position and add all four slots: _______

Paper 3 — Global Challenges Introduction (HL Only)

1h 30m · 28 Marks · Focus: 15-mark extended response (Question 3)

Step 1 — Learn the Formula

Paper 3 presents a stimulus and a Question 3 drawn from a global political challenge topic area. Your introduction must: restate the question in your own terms, explicitly name the challenge topic area(s) you will use, identify key and related concepts, preview your case studies, signal a counterclaim, and take a clear evaluative position — all while using the language of Question 3 and the language of the stimulus.

Q3 ≠ Generic Essay Unlike Paper 2, Q3 is always anchored to a specific global political challenge topic area (e.g., Climate Change & Environmental Sustainability, Global Health, Forced Migration, Poverty & Inequality, Nuclear Proliferation, Terrorism, Cybersecurity, Resource Scarcity). Name it explicitly in your introduction.
Slot 1Q Restatement + Challenge Framing
+
Slot 2Perspectives & Counterclaim
+
Slot 3Evaluative Thesis (Q3 Language)
1

Question Restatement + Challenge Framing

Restate the question in your own words. Name the global political challenge topic area (e.g., Climate Change & Environmental Sustainability). Introduce the key concept (e.g., interdependence) and a related concept (e.g., human security). Reference the stimulus to anchor your response, then add your own data to demonstrate independent knowledge. Give brief introductory information about the case studies you will use and why they are relevant to the political issue.

2

Perspectives & Counterclaim

Identify the perspectives and positions different actors take on the political issue raised by the question. Present one side of the argument, then signal the counterclaim — the contrasting view you will engage with. This shows the examiner you understand the debate, not just one side of it. Show the power dynamics between those who shape the issue and those who bear its consequences.

3

Evaluative Thesis (echoing Q3 Language)

State your clear position — which side, on the balance of the evidence, is more powerful and why. Echo the exact language of Question 3 (e.g., if the Q asks about "effectiveness of international cooperation," use those words in your thesis). Name the challenge topic areas that will frame your evaluation. Use: "This response will argue that while [optimist view]…, the effectiveness of [Q3 term] is fundamentally constrained by [reason] because…"

Step 2 — Read the Stimulus Material

Stimulus — Climate Finance and the Global South

"At COP28 in Dubai (2023), developed nations pledged to operationalise a 'Loss and Damage' fund for climate-vulnerable countries. However, by mid-2024, only $700 million had been committed — a fraction of the estimated $400 billion needed annually. Small Island Developing States (SIDS) like Tuvalu and the Marshall Islands face existential threats from rising sea levels, yet contribute less than 0.03% of global emissions. Meanwhile, the world's top 10 emitters — responsible for over 70% of greenhouse gases — have resisted binding emissions targets, preferring voluntary nationally determined contributions (NDCs) under the Paris Agreement.

The disparity between pledges and action extends beyond the Loss and Damage fund. The Green Climate Fund, established in 2010 with a target of mobilising $100 billion annually by 2020, did not reach that threshold until 2023 — three years late — and much of the finance was delivered as loans rather than grants, increasing the debt burden of already vulnerable nations. Meanwhile, climate litigation is emerging as an alternative pathway: in 2024, the International Court of Justice received a historic advisory opinion request from Pacific Island states on the legal obligations of major emitters, and Vanuatu's campaign for climate justice at the UN General Assembly garnered support from over 130 nations. These developments suggest that when multilateral frameworks fail to deliver, affected states are increasingly turning to international law and grassroots mobilisation to force accountability."

— Synthesised from UNEP Emissions Gap Report 2024, COP28 outcome documents, and ICJ proceedings 2024

Predicted Prompt (Question 3): "With reference to one of the HL topic areas, discuss the view that international frameworks for addressing global political challenges systematically favour the interests of powerful states over the most-affected populations." [15 marks]
Suggested HL Topic Area: Climate Change & Environmental Sustainability
Note: this is a teacher-predicted format based on published IB specimen materials — the first examination is May 2026.

Step 3 — Study the High-Scoring Example

Notice how this intro: restates the Q3 language (discuss the view that… frameworks… systematically favour…) → names the HL topic area → introduces key + related concepts → previews two case studies → signals a counterclaim with stimulus evidence → ends with a thesis that echoes the exact Q3 wording.
Predicted High-Scoring Paper 3 Intro (Q3)

The view that international frameworks for addressing global political challenges systematically favour the interests of powerful states over the most-affected populations is starkly illustrated by the stimulus: despite commitments at COP28, only $700 million of an estimated $400 billion annual need was mobilised for climate-vulnerable nations, while the world's top ten emitters — responsible for over 70% of greenhouse gas emissions — continue to resist binding targets. Within the HL topic area of Climate Change & Environmental Sustainability, the key concept of power — specifically who holds agenda-setting authority in multilateral institutions — and the related concept of human security are essential to evaluating this claim. This response will draw on the cases of the Green Climate Fund's loan-heavy disbursement model and Vanuatu's ICJ advisory opinion campaign to assess the extent to which existing frameworks serve the most-affected states. Those who challenge this view point to the Paris Agreement as evidence that international frameworks can mobilise unprecedented normative consensus — 196 signatories represent a broader coalition than any previous climate regime — suggesting that powerful states have at least accepted the principle of collective responsibility. However, a more critical reading of the stimulus reveals a structural asymmetry: the Green Climate Fund delivered much of its finance as loans rather than grants, deepening the debt burden of already-vulnerable nations, while the major emitters who shape these frameworks have consistently subordinated binding obligations to national economic interests. This response will discuss the view that, while international frameworks have created important normative architecture, they systematically favour the interests of powerful states by embedding voluntarist compliance, asymmetric finance mechanisms, and state-centric governance structures that structurally marginalise the most-affected populations from decision-making.

Step 4 — Diagnose Weak Introductions

Below are four poorly written introductions for the same predicted Q3 prompt and stimulus. For each one, identify the missing formula slots and write what you would add to fix it.

"At COP28, developed nations pledged to create a Loss and Damage fund. Only $700 million was committed. SIDS like Tuvalu face threats from sea level rise. The top 10 emitters are responsible for 70% of greenhouse gases. Therefore, international frameworks clearly favour powerful states."
Diagnosis: Restates the stimulus almost verbatim with zero independent knowledge — Q3 requires you to go beyond the stimulus. The opening does not restate the Q3 language ("systematically favour the interests of powerful states" should appear early and drive the analysis). No HL topic area is named, no key or related concepts are introduced, no case studies are previewed, and there is no Counterclaim. The conclusion simply lifts the question as a statement, which is not an evaluative Thesis.
Your fix — Add a counterclaim, name the HL topic area, introduce a concept, and rewrite the thesis to discuss (not assert): ____
"Climate change is one of the most serious global challenges. The Paris Agreement was signed in 2015 by nearly 200 countries. Despite this, many countries have failed to meet their targets. Powerful countries like the US and China have a lot of influence. This essay will discuss whether international frameworks favour powerful states."
Diagnosis: Makes no reference to the stimulus at all — Q3 specifically requires it. The Question Restatement doesn't echo Q3 language precisely ("most-affected populations" is entirely absent). No HL topic area is named, no concepts are identified, no case studies are previewed, and the final sentence is a description of intent rather than a genuine Perspectives & Counterclaim or Evaluative Thesis.
Your fix — Reference the stimulus data, name the HL topic area, signal the counterclaim, and state your position: ____
"There are many actors involved in climate change frameworks: the UN, the IPCC, the World Bank, the US, China, the EU, Small Island States, NGOs, and fossil fuel lobbies. Each has different interests. As the stimulus shows, some countries have committed more money than others. Frameworks are important for solving climate change."
Diagnosis: Lists nine actors without showing the power dynamics or positions between them — the Perspectives slot must show who argues what and why, not simply who exists. The stimulus is referenced vaguely. No HL topic area is named, no key concepts are introduced, no case studies are previewed, no counterclaim is signalled, and the final sentence is not an Evaluative Thesis — it simply states that frameworks matter.
Your fix — Pick 2 actors, show the power dynamic between them, and argue a clear position: ____
"International climate frameworks have not worked well for developing countries. Rich nations have more power and this creates unfair outcomes. The Paris Agreement and COP meetings show that big countries control the agenda. This essay will argue that climate governance needs to be reformed to be fairer."
Diagnosis: This introduction never echoes the exact language of Question 3. Words like "discuss," "systematically favour," "the interests of powerful states," and "most-affected populations" are absent. No HL topic area is named, no key concepts (power? human security?) are identified, and no case study is previewed. While there is a vague Counterclaim signal, it names no specific actors or evidence. The thesis ("needs to be reformed") is a normative recommendation, not a discussive evaluative claim.
Your fix — Echo the Q3 wording, name the HL topic area, add a concept and case study preview, and give a nuanced thesis: ____
Examiner Reminder — The Two-Language Rule (IB Specimen Textbook) "Make sure that you are using the language of Question 3 as well as the language of the stimulus throughout your response." This means your thesis must echo the exact wording of the Q3 prompt. For this predicted question, phrases like "systematically favour," "most-affected populations," and "discuss the view" should appear in your introduction — anchored to evidence from the stimulus and your own case studies.
