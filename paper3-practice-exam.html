"Green Colonization": Renewable Energy and Self-Determination in Western Sahara

Morocco has accelerated plans to build Africa's largest green hydrogen facility — the "White Dunes" project — in occupied Western Sahara. The $11.6 billion development near Dakhla would generate 8 GW of combined solar and wind capacity for electrolysis-based hydrogen production. A 1,400 km high-voltage transmission line connecting the territory to Casablanca is under construction, scheduled for completion in 2027.

The expansion occurs in a territory the UN classifies as non-self-governing. Since Morocco's 1975 annexation, the International Court of Justice has found no sovereign ties between Morocco and Western Sahara. Yet by 2025, at least 81% of land designated for new renewable concessions fell within the occupied territory, according to Western Sahara Resource Watch (WSRW).

"We are witnessing the birth of a new form of colonialism. The occupier tells the world it is saving the planet while extracting the resources of a people who never consented. Green energy should not be built on stolen land." — Mohamed Dihani, Sahrawi human rights activist, 2025
"Morocco's renewable energy strategy positions the Kingdom as a global leader in the green transition. The projects in our southern provinces create jobs, attract investment, and contribute to the UN Sustainable Development Goals." — Leila Benali, Moroccan Minister of Energy Transition, 2024

In 2024, the EU's Court of Justice annulled trade and fishing agreements between the EU and Morocco insofar as they applied to Western Sahara, ruling that Sahrawi consent was required. Despite this, European energy firms including AMEA Power continue to negotiate offtake agreements for green hydrogen produced in the territory. The Polisario Front has called for an international boycott of all products derived from Sahrawi resources.

✅ Model Answer — Q2b (6/6 marks · Scholar Tier)
MARK = criteria met AMR² = formula element

ACTOR 1/6 — specific actor The European Commission, acting through its Directorate-General for Energy (DG ENER), should implement a mandatory origin-of-energy tracing requirement for all green hydrogen imports entering the EU single market.

MECHANISM 2-3/6 — specific, actionable mechanism Specifically, the Commission should amend the EU's Delegated Act on Renewable Hydrogen (2023) to require that all imported hydrogen carry a certificate of geographic origin verified by an independent third-party auditor. Any hydrogen produced on land classified by the UN as non-self-governing territory — including Western Sahara — would be ineligible for the EU's green taxonomy and thus blocked from carbon credit markets. This directly enforces the EU Court of Justice's 2024 ruling that trade agreements cannot extend to Western Sahara without Sahrawi consent.

RATIONALE 4/6 — justified with evidence This policy is feasible because the EU already operates a robust traceability system for agricultural products under Protected Designation of Origin (PDO) regulations — extending this to energy imports is a technical, not legal, challenge. The political rationale is that the EU's own court has established the legal basis; failure to enforce it exposes the EU to accusations of selective application of international law. Furthermore, the EU's Global Gateway strategy explicitly links energy partnerships to human rights conditionality — this policy aligns rhetoric with practice.

RISK 5/6 — identifies risk However, this policy risks disrupting the EU's Green Deal timeline, as Morocco is currently the EU's closest and cheapest potential hydrogen supplier. Blocking Western Sahara-origin hydrogen could remove up to 8 GW of planned capacity from the EU's supply projections, creating a short-term supply gap that would raise hydrogen prices and potentially delay industrial decarbonization targets.

MITIGATION 6/6 — proposes mitigation To mitigate this, the Commission should simultaneously fast-track Memoranda of Understanding with alternative hydrogen exporters — particularly Namibia (whose Hyphen project is already EU-supported), Chile (Southern Hemisphere wind advantage), and Algeria (existing gas pipeline infrastructure convertible to hydrogen). This diversification would reduce dependency on any single supplier and insulate the Green Deal from politically unstable sourcing. SCHOLAR TIER

Introduction Defines key terms

"Sustainable development" — as defined by the 1987 Brundtland Commission — is development that "meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs." "Self-determination" is the right of peoples to freely determine their political status and pursue their economic, social, and cultural development, enshrined in Article 1 of the UN Charter and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights. The central tension is this: can development truly be sustainable when the people most affected have no political mechanism to shape it? Clear thesis I argue that self-determination is a necessary but not sufficient condition for sustainable development — Western Sahara illustrates why its absence guarantees exploitation, while cases like Haiti show that formal sovereignty alone does not guarantee development.

Body 1 — FOR the claim: Western Sahara as evidence that development without self-determination is extraction Uses stimulus evidence

The stimulus demonstrates that Morocco's $11.6 billion "White Dunes" green hydrogen project exemplifies development that serves external interests while bypassing the political agency of the occupied Sahrawi population. The 81% statistic — showing that the vast majority of Morocco's new renewable concessions fall within non-self-governing territory — reveals a structural logic: the "development" Morocco claims to bring is located on land where political resistance is systematically suppressed. Sahrawi human rights activist Mohamed Dihani calls this "a new form of colonialism," and the framing is not hyperbolic. Engages critically with stimulus

This connects directly to the GPC of Equality. The barrier to political, economic, and social equality here is not poverty or lack of resources — Western Sahara has abundant solar, wind, and phosphate wealth. The barrier is the absence of self-determination: without it, the Sahrawi people cannot control how their resources are used, who benefits, and under what terms. The EU Court of Justice's 2024 ruling confirms this — trade agreements cannot legally extend to Western Sahara without Sahrawi consent — yet enforcement remains absent, exposing the limits of the liberal international legal order. Links to GPC Uses theory implicitly

Body 2 — AGAINST the claim: Self-determination alone does not guarantee sustainable development Nuance / counter-argument

However, formal self-determination is not sufficient. Haiti achieved independence in 1804 — the first successful slave revolution in history — yet it is the poorest country in the Western Hemisphere. Haiti's sovereign status did not prevent 200 years of external interference: the French "independence debt," US occupations (1915-34), and ongoing foreign-led interventions culminating in the 2024-2025 Kenya-led Multinational Security Support Mission. Haiti's development challenges — where 5,600 people were killed in 2024 alone — demonstrate that self-determination without institutional capacity, equitable trade relationships, and freedom from neocolonial control remains hollow. Uses own case study knowledge

This complicates the claim: self-determination is necessary but not sufficient. A state can be formally sovereign yet structurally dependent — what dependency theorists call "underdevelopment" — where unequal integration into the global economy ensures that the surplus value of a nation's resources flows outward. Uses theory explicitly

Body 3 — Synthesis: Technology creates new forms of the self-determination problem Links multiple GPCs

The GPC of Technology adds a further dimension. Green technology — solar panels, wind turbines, hydrogen electrolysis — was supposed to democratize energy production and reduce reliance on fossil-fuel extractivism. Yet the Western Sahara case shows that the same power asymmetries persist: whoever controls the technology, the land, and the export infrastructure captures the value. From a constructivist perspective, the discursive framing of these projects as "green" and "sustainable" performs a legitimizing function — it makes colonial extraction appear progressive. The label "renewable energy" socially constructs the project as beyond political criticism, when in reality it reproduces the precise dynamic that fossil fuel extraction exhibited: powerful actors exploiting resources on land they do not legitimately control, for markets they exclusively access. Theory application Constructivist analysis

Conclusion Evaluates 'to what extent'

"To a large extent," the claim is valid. Self-determination is a necessary — though not independently sufficient — condition for sustainable development. Without it, as Western Sahara shows, development is structurally colonial: resources are extracted, value is exported, and the people whose land is used are excluded from both the process and the benefit. However, Haiti demonstrates that formal self-determination must be accompanied by structural conditions — equitable trade, institutional integrity, and freedom from external political interference — to produce genuinely sustainable outcomes. The most dangerous development model is the one that disguises extraction as sustainability, which is precisely what "green colonization" represents. Until the international community enforces the legal and moral right of peoples to control their own resources — whether through the UN, the ICJ, or trade conditionality — sustainable development will remain a privilege of the powerful, not a universal right.
